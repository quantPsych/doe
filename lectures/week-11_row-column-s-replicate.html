Introduction to s-replicate Latin Square Designs

A Latin square design is an experimental design that controls for two nuisance variables (row and column blocks) while evaluating the effect of a treatment variable. The \(s\)-replicate Latin square extends this concept by replicating a \(v \times v\) square \(s\) times such that each row is replicated \(s\) times, balancing treatments across rows and columns. In other words, the \(s\)-replicate design concatenates a \(v \times v\) square vertically.

  • Key Parameters:
    • \(v\): Number of treatments.
    • \(s\): Number of replicates.
    • Number of rows: \(b = v s\).
    • Number of columns: \(c = v\).
    • Total observations: \(N = b \times c = v^2 s\).
    • Treatment frequency: Each of the \(v\) treatments appears \(r = v s\) times, once per row and \(s\) times per column.

Replication of Latin Squares and Orthogonality in Experimental Designs

In experimental design, a single Latin square of order \(v\) provides \(r = v\) observations per treatment. However, in many practical situations—especially when the number of treatments is small—this number of replications may be insufficient for reliable statistical inference. One common solution is to replicate the Latin square by piecing together \(s\) separate \(v \times v\) Latin squares. The resulting design, known as an \(s\)-replicate Latin square, increases the number of observations per treatment to

\[ r = s \times v. \]

Because the replications are arranged in a larger block, the row or column blocks in the combined design become larger, typically of size \(s \times v\).

Below, we explain the concept in detail using different examples, illustrate the construction with tables, and discuss the orthogonality of these designs.

Basic Concept of Replication

  • A standard Latin square design of order \(v\) has \(v\) rows and \(v\) columns, with each treatment appearing exactly once in each row and each column.
  • If \(v = 3\), a single Latin square would provide 3 observations per treatment.
  • To increase replication, we can “stack” multiple Latin squares. For example, if we replicate a 3×3 Latin square twice (\(s = 2\)), each treatment is observed

\[ r = 2 \times 3 = 6 \text{ times.} \]

Stacking can be done in different ways depending on how the rows (or columns) from each square are arranged in the final design. The choice of arrangement can affect the properties of the design, particularly the ability to estimate interaction effects or contrasts independently of the blocking factors.

Example: 2-Replicate Latin Squares for \(v = 3\)

Consider two different plans for constructing a 2-replicate Latin square for \(v = 3\).

Plan 1

In Plan 1, the same ordering of the Latin square is simply repeated twice. The combined design is as follows:

Row Column 1 Column 2 Column 3
1 A B C
2 B C A
3 C A B
4 A B C
5 B C A
6 C A B

Here, each treatment appears 6 times overall (3 times per replicate). However, notice that the row orderings in both replicates are identical. This lack of variation in the row block orderings can limit the capability of the design to fully disentangle certain interaction contrasts.

Plan 2

Plan 2 uses a different ordering for the second Latin square to enhance balance. One way to achieve this is to change the order in which treatments appear in the second square. For instance:

Row Column 1 Column 2 Column 3
1 A B C
2 B C A
3 C A B
4 A C B
5 B A C
6 C B A

In Plan 2, although each treatment is still replicated 6 times, the row blocks now consist of every possible ordering of the three treatments. This variation in the ordering of treatments in the row blocks improves the structure of the design by ensuring that all potential row (or subject) ordering effects are represented in the final analysis. Even after randomization, the design retains this balance, which is highly desirable for the estimation of treatment and interaction effects.

Orthogonality of the Designs

Definition of Orthogonality

In the context of experimental design, orthogonality refers to the property that the estimates of the effects (such as treatment, row, and column effects) are statistically independent. In mathematical terms, if the design matrix columns corresponding to different factors are orthogonal (i.e., their inner product is zero), then the estimation of one effect does not influence the estimation of another. This leads to simpler analysis and clearer interpretation of the treatment contrasts.

Orthogonality in Latin Square Designs

  • In a standard Latin square, the structure itself ensures that each treatment appears exactly once in each row and column. This balance leads to an inherent orthogonality between treatments and the two blocking factors (rows and columns).
  • When Latin squares are replicated (as in the \(s\)-replicate design), the orthogonality can be maintained for the main effects if the replicates are chosen carefully.

Impact of Replication on Orthogonality

  • Plan 1 vs. Plan 2 for \(v = 3\): Although both plans achieve the desired replication level (6 observations per treatment), they differ in how well they support the estimation of interaction contrasts—specifically, the column-treatment interaction.
    • Plan 1: The identical ordering in the row blocks across the two replicates means that not all potential interaction contrasts are estimable in an orthogonal fashion. The resulting structure does not provide a full set of \((v-1)^2\) orthogonal contrasts for the column-treatment interaction.
    • Plan 2: The deliberate change in ordering for the second replicate (yielding all possible orderings of treatments in the row blocks) allows the design to achieve a full set of \((v-1)^2\) orthogonal contrasts for the adjusted column-treatment interaction. For \(v = 3\), this full set amounts to 4 degrees of freedom.
  • General Considerations: Replicating a Latin square design by stacking \(s\) Latin squares introduces an extra layer of structure (the replicate factor). To maintain overall orthogonality:
    • The ordering of treatments across replicates should be arranged so that the additional block (the replicate) does not confound treatment contrasts.
    • When done correctly, the design allows independent estimation of treatment effects, block (row and column) effects, and, in some cases, interaction contrasts.

Summary of Orthogonality

  • Orthogonal Designs: A design is considered orthogonal if the main effects (and any estimable interaction effects) can be estimated independently—that is, the design matrix has orthogonal columns corresponding to the different factors.
  • In Replicated Latin Squares: Not all replications guarantee complete orthogonality. For instance, in the \(2\)-replicate Latin square example for \(v = 3\):
    • Plan 2 is orthogonal with respect to the column-treatment interaction, providing the full set of \((v-1)^2\) contrasts.
    • Plan 1 lacks this property, meaning that some contrasts are confounded or not independently estimable.

Choosing the right replication plan is crucial in experimental design, especially when interaction effects (such as column-treatment contrasts) are of interest.

The Row–Column–Treatment Model

The statistical model for an \(s\)-replicate Latin square is:

\[ y_{hqi} = \mu + \theta_h + \phi_q + \tau_i + \epsilon_{hqi} \]

Where:

  • \(y_{hqi}\): Observation for treatment \(i\) in row \(h\), column \(q\).
  • \(\mu\): Overall mean.
  • \(\theta_h\): Effect of row block \(h\) (\(h = 1, \ldots, v s\)).
  • \(\phi_q\): Effect of column block \(q\) (\(q = 1, \ldots, v\)).
  • \(\tau_i\): Effect of treatment \(i\) (\(i = 1, \ldots, v\)).
  • \(\epsilon_{hqi}\): Random error, assumed \(\epsilon_{hqi} \sim N(0, \sigma^2)\).

Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)

The ANOVA partitions the total variability into components due to rows, columns, treatments, and error.

For an s-replicate Latin square design, every treatment appears once in every row block and \(s\) times in every column block, so there are \(b= vs\) rows, \(c = v\) columns, and we have \(n_{h.i} = 1\), \(n_{q.i} = s\).

Sums of Squares

  • Total Sum of Squares (\(SS_\text{Total}\)):

\[ SS_\text{Total} = \sum_{h=1}^{vs} \sum_{q=1}^v (y_{hqi} - \bar{y}_{…})^2 \]

  • Row Sum of Squares (\(SS_\text{Row}\)):

\[ \begin{align*} SS_\text{Rows} &= v \sum_{h=1}^{vs} (\bar{y}_{h..} - \bar{y}_{…})^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{v} \sum_{h=1}^{b} B_h^2 - \frac{G^2}{vs} \end{align*} \]

Where: - \(B_h\): Sum of observations for row \(h\). - \(\bar{y}_{h..}\): Mean of observations in row \(h\). - \(\bar{y}_{…}\): Overall mean of all observations.

  • Column Sum of Squares (\(SS_\text{Col}\)):

\[ \begin{align*} SS_\text{Col} &= v s \sum_{q=1}^v (\bar{y}_{.q.} - \bar{y}_{…})^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{b} \sum_{q=1}^{c} C_q^2 - \frac{G^2}{bc} \end{align*} \]

Where:

  • \(C_q\): Sum of observations for column \(q\).

  • \(\bar{y}_{.q.}\): Mean of observations in column \(q\).

  • Treatment Sum of Squares (\(SS_\text{Total adj}\)):

\[ \hat{\tau}_i = \frac{1}{vs} Q_i \]

Where:

\[ Q_i= T_i - \frac{1}{c} \sum_{h=1}^{vs} n_{h.i} B_h= T_i - \frac{G}{v} \]

\[ SS_\text{Total adj} = \frac{1}{v s} \sum_{i=1}^v T_i^2 - \frac{G^2}{v^2 s} \]

Where:

  • \(T_i\): Sum of observations for treatment \(i\).

  • \(G\): Grand total of all observations.

  • Error Sum of Squares (\(SS_\text{Error}\)):

\[ SS_\text{Error} = SS_\text{Total} - SS_\text{Row} - SS_\text{Col} - SS_\text{Total adj} \]

Degrees of Freedom

  • Total: \(v^2 s - 1\)
  • Rows: \(v s - 1\)
  • Columns: \(v - 1\)
  • Treatments: \(v - 1\)
  • Error: \((v s - 2)(v - 1)\)

Mean Squares

  • \(MS_\text{Row} = \frac{SS_\text{Row}}{v s - 1}\)
  • \(MS_\text{Col} = \frac{SS_\text{Col}}{v - 1}\)
  • \(MS_\text{Treatment adj} = \frac{SS_\text{Total adj}}{v - 1}\)
  • \(MS_\text{Error} = \frac{SS_\text{Error}}{(v s - 2)(v - 1)}\)

Testing Treatment Effects

  • Null Hypothesis (\(H_0\)): \(\tau_1 = \tau_2 = \cdots = \tau_v\).
  • Test Statistic:

\[ F = \frac{MS_\text{Treatment adj}}{MS_\text{Error}} \]

  • Critical Value: Compare to \(F_{v-1, (v s - 2)(v - 1), \alpha}\).
  • Decision: Reject \(H_0\) if \(F\) exceeds the critical value, indicating significant treatment differences.

Evaluating Blocking Effectiveness

  • Compare \(MS_\text{Row}\) and \(MS_\text{Col}\) to \(MS_\text{Error}\):
    • If \(MS_\text{Row} > MS_\text{Error}\) or \(MS_\text{Col} > MS_\text{Error}\), blocking reduced experimental error.
    • No adjustment to row or column sums of squares is needed due to the design’s balance.

Example: Dairy Cow Experiment

Experiment Description

  • Objective: Study the effect of three diets (roughage, limited grain, full grain) on milk yield.
  • Design: 2-replicate Latin square (\(v = 3\), \(s = 2\)).
    • Rows: \(b = v s = 6\) cows.
    • Columns: \(c = v = 3\) periods.
    • Total observations: \(N = v^2 s = 18\).
    • Each diet appears \(r = v s = 6\) times.

Computations

  • Treatment Totals:
    • \(T_1\) (A): \(63 + 46 + 109 + 35 + 38 + 27 = 318\)
    • \(T_2\) (B): \(101 + 76 + 39 + 34 + 25 + 124 = 399\)
    • \(T_3\) (C): \(1 + 86 + 86 + 75 + 15 + 72 = 335\)
    • \(G = 318 + 399 + 335 = 1052\)
  • \(SS_\text{Total adj}\):

\[ SS_\text{Total adj} = \frac{1}{6} (318^2 + 399^2 + 335^2) - \frac{1052^2}{18} = 2276.78 \]

  • Test Statistic:

\[ F = \frac{1138.39}{103.06} = 11.05 \]

  • Critical value: \(F_{2, 8, 0.01} = 8.65\).
  • Since \(11.05 > 8.65\), reject \(H_0\) at \(\alpha = 0.01\).
  • Conclusion: Diets have significantly different effects on milk yield (p = 0.0050).
  • Blocking Effectiveness: \(MS_\text{Row} = 1156.22\) and \(MS_\text{Col} = 5740.06\) both exceed \(MS_\text{Error} = 103.06\), confirming blocking reduced error.

Confidence Intervals and Multiple Comparisons

Estimable Contrasts

All treatment contrasts \(\sum d_i \tau_i\) (where \(\sum d_i = 0\)) are estimable. The least squares estimate is:

\[ \sum d_i \hat{\tau}_i = \frac{1}{v s} \sum d_i T_i \]

Variance:

\[ \text{Var}\left( \sum d_i \hat{\tau}_i \right) = \frac{\sum d_i^2}{v s} \sigma^2 \]

Confidence Intervals

Using Tukey’s method:

\[ \left( \frac{1}{v s} \sum d_i T_i \right) \pm w_T \sqrt{ MS_\text{Error} \cdot \frac{\sum d_i^2}{v s} } \]

Where \(w_T = \frac{q_{v, (v s - 2)(v - 1), \alpha}}{\sqrt{2}}\).

Dairy Cow Example (Continued)

  • Treatment Means:
    • \(\bar{y}_{1..} = \frac{318}{6} = 45.167\)
    • \(\bar{y}_{2..} = \frac{399}{6} = 57.500\)
    • \(\bar{y}_{3..} = \frac{335}{6} = 72.667\)
  • Pairwise Differences:
    • \(\hat{\tau}_2 - \hat{\tau}_1 = 57.500 - 45.167 = 12.333\)
    • \(\hat{\tau}_3 - \hat{\tau}_1 = 72.667 - 45.167 = 27.500\)
    • \(\hat{\tau}_3 - \hat{\tau}_2 = 72.667 - 57.500 = 15.167\)
  • Tukey’s 95% CI:
    • \(w_T = \frac{q_{3, 8, 0.05}}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{4.04}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 2.857\)
    • For pairwise comparisons, \(\sum d_i^2 = 2\), \(v s = 6\):
    • Minimum significant difference:

\[ \text{msd} = 2.857 \sqrt{103.06 \cdot \frac{2}{6}} = 16.743 \]

  • Intervals:
    • \(\tau_3 - \tau_1\): \(27.500 \pm 16.743 = (10.58, 44.24)\)
    • \(\tau_2 - \tau_1\): \(12.333 \pm 16.743 = (-4.41, 29.08)\)
    • \(\tau_3 - \tau_2\): \(15.167 \pm 16.743 = (-1.58, 31.91)\)
  • Conclusion: Diet 3 (full grain) significantly increases milk yield over Diet 1 (roughage). Diet 2 (limited grain) is not significantly different from either.

Summary

  • Advantages:
    • Controls two sources of variability (rows and columns).
    • Orthogonal structure simplifies analysis.
    • Efficient use of experimental units.
  • Key Points:
    • Use \(SS_\text{Total adj}\) to \(SS_\text{Error}\) treatment effects.
    • Evaluate blocking by comparing mean squares.
    • Apply multiple comparison methods (e.g., Tukey’s) for detailed insights.

This lecture note provides a thorough understanding of \(s\)-replicate Latin square designs, blending theory with a practical example from the dairy cow experiment.

What is MSD?

The Minimum Significant Difference (MSD) is a statistical threshold used in multiple comparison procedures, such as Tukey’s method, to determine the smallest difference between two treatment means that can be considered statistically significant at a specified confidence level (e.g., 95%). In the context of an \(s\)-replicate Latin square design, MSD helps identify which pairs of treatments differ significantly after finding a significant treatment effect in the ANOVA.

  • Purpose: MSD provides a practical way to interpret pairwise differences by setting a benchmark. If the observed difference between two treatment means exceeds the MSD, we conclude the difference is statistically significant.
  • Context: It’s particularly useful in designs like the Latin square, where treatments are compared while controlling for two blocking factors (rows and columns).

For Tukey’s method, the MSD is calculated using:

  • The studentized range statistic (\(q\)), which accounts for multiple comparisons.
  • The error mean square (\(MS_\text{Error}\)) from the ANOVA, representing residual variability.
  • The number of observations per treatment (\(v s\)), reflecting the design’s replication.

Formula for MSD

For pairwise comparisons in an \(s\)-replicate Latin square:

\[ \text{MSD} = w_T \sqrt{ MS_\text{Error} \cdot \frac{2}{v s} } \]

Where:

  • \(w_T = \frac{q_{v, df_{\text{error}}, \alpha}}{\sqrt{2}}\):
    • \(q_{v, df_{\text{error}}, \alpha}\): Critical value from the studentized range distribution, based on \(v\) (number of treatments), \(df_{\text{error}}\) (error degrees of freedom), and \(\alpha\) (significance level).
    • The \(\sqrt{2}\) adjusts \(q\) to the scale of a t-statistic for pairwise differences.
  • \(MS_\text{Error}\): Error mean square from the ANOVA.
  • \(v s\): Number of times each treatment is replicated (\(v\) is the number of treatments, \(s\) is the number of replicates).
  • \(\frac{2}{v s}\): Variance factor for the difference between two means, since \(\text{Var}(\bar{y}_{i..} - \bar{y}_{j..}) = \sigma^2 \cdot \frac{2}{v s}\).

The MSD represents the margin of error around a pairwise difference. If the absolute difference between two treatment means exceeds the MSD, we reject the null hypothesis of no difference at the specified \(\alpha\) level.

Example: Dairy Cow Experiment

Let’s apply this to a 2-replicate Latin square design studying the effect of three diets (A: roughage, B: limited grain, C: full grain) on milk yield.

Design Details

  • \(v = 3\) (treatments: A, B, C).
  • \(s = 2\) (replicates).
  • Rows: \(b = v s = 6\) (cows).
  • Columns: \(c = v = 3\) (periods).
  • Total observations: \(N = v^2 s = 18\).
  • Treatment frequency: \(r = v s = 6\).

Treatment Means

  • Diet A: \(\frac{63 + 46 + 109 + 35 + 38 + 27}{6} = 45.167\)
  • Diet B: \(\frac{101 + 76 + 39 + 34 + 25 + 124}{6} = 57.500\)
  • Diet C: \(\frac{1 + 86 + 86 + 75 + 15 + 72}{6} = 72.667\)

Manual MSD Calculation

  • For \(\alpha = 0.05\), \(v = 3\), \(df_{\text{error}} = 8\):
    • \(q_{3, 8, 0.05} \approx 4.04\) (from studentized range tables).
    • \(w_T = \frac{4.04}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 2.857\).
  • Compute MSD:

\[ \text{MSD} = 2.857 \sqrt{103.06 \cdot \frac{2}{6}} = 2.857 \sqrt{103.06 \cdot \frac{1}{3}} = 2.857 \sqrt{34.353} \approx 2.857 \times 5.861 \approx 16.743 \]

Pairwise Differences

  • B–A: \(57.500 - 45.167 = 12.333\)
  • C–A: \(72.667 - 45.167 = 27.500\)
  • B–C: \(57.500 - 72.667 = -15.167\)
