Introduction

In many experiments, researchers compare multiple treatments or conditions to understand their effects on a response variable. For example, consider a horticulturalist testing various fertilizers on plant growth or a biologist examining different drugs on patient outcomes. After performing an ANOVA and finding evidence that not all treatment means are equal, the next step is to determine which treatments differ, and by how much.

Multiple Comparison Procedures (MCPs) and contrasts address this challenge:

  • MCPs control the overall probability of false positives (Type I errors) when making many comparisons.
  • Contrasts focus on specific, pre-defined linear combinations of treatment means, often reflecting scientific questions.

The combination of MCPs and contrasts ensures valid inferences, mitigating the risk of drawing erroneous conclusions from multiple tests while retaining interpretability and power.

Objectives

  1. Understand the rationale for performing multiple comparisons after ANOVA.
  2. Explore common MCPs: Fisher’s LSD, Tukey’s HSD, Bonferroni, and Scheffé methods.
  3. Learn how to construct orthogonal and non-orthogonal contrasts and interpret their results.
  4. Utilize R to perform multiple comparisons and test contrasts on real or simulated data.
  5. Discuss Type I error control, power considerations, and the trade-offs in choosing different procedures.
  6. Develop deeper theoretical insights (in the Appendix) and practice through exercises of medium and challenging difficulty.

Motivation and Context

Imagine testing four battery types (A, B, C, D) to see which lasts longest. After ANOVA suggests at least one mean differs, you want to pinpoint where differences lie:

  • Are A and B different?
  • Is C worse than the average of A and B?
  • Does D stand apart?

Without controlling for multiple testing, repeated pairwise comparisons inflate the risk of false positives. MCPs handle this by adjusting significance criteria, while contrasts let you test targeted hypotheses, such as comparing the average performance of two treatments against another group’s mean.

Analogies

  • Mail Sorting Analogy: Consider sorting through a large pile of mail (treatment comparisons). Without rules (MCPs), you might mistakenly pick “important” letters (differences) that are actually junk (Type I errors). MCPs act like filters, reducing the chance you misclassify too many letters as important.
  • Magnifying Glass for Specific Questions (Contrasts): Instead of looking at all letters at once, a contrast is like using a magnifying glass to focus on a subset of letters (treatment means) to answer a specific question.

Contrasts: Definition and Properties

What is a Contrast?

A contrast is a linear combination of treatment means that sums to zero:

\[ L = \sum_{i=1}^{v} c_i \mu_i, \quad \text{with } \sum_{i=1}^{v} c_i = 0. \]

This ensures we’re measuring relative differences. Examples:

  • Pairwise difference: \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\).
  • Comparison of groups: \(\frac{\mu_1 + \mu_2}{2} - \frac{\mu_3 + \mu_4}{2}\).

Variance of a Contrast

If each treatment \(i\) has \(n_i\) observations and errors are \(N(0,\sigma^2)\), then:

\[ \text{Var}(L) = \sigma^2 \sum_{i=1}^v \frac{c_i^2}{n_i}. \]

This variance helps form confidence intervals and tests for the contrast.

Example

In a battery life study with treatments A, B, C, D, suppose we suspect that (A and B) as a group differ from (C and D). We might form a contrast:

\[ L = \frac{\mu_A + \mu_B}{2} - \frac{\mu_C + \mu_D}{2}. \]

This contrast directly tests a scientific hypothesis: Do the top two battery types outperform the bottom two on average?

Multiple Comparison Procedures (MCPs)

After ANOVA, MCPs help identify which treatments differ while controlling the Type I error rate across all comparisons:

Fisher’s Least Significant Difference (LSD)

  • Conduct an ANOVA test first. If significant, proceed.
  • Compare pairwise means using the standard t-test but do not adjust alpha for multiple tests.
  • Pros: Simple, more powerful.
  • Cons: Higher chance of Type I errors when many comparisons are made.

Tukey’s Honest Significant Difference (HSD)

  • Designed for pairwise comparisons.
  • Controls the family-wise error rate (FWER).
  • For equal sample sizes:

\[ \text{HSD} = q_{\alpha,v,n-v} \sqrt{\frac{\text{MSE}}{r}}, \]

where \(q\) is from the studentized range distribution, \(v\) is the number of groups, \(n\) is the total sample size, and \(r\) is the number of observations per group.

For a more detailed explanation of Tukey’s HSD, see the tutorial page Tukey’s HSD in the appendix.

Tukey’s HSD Procedure

  1. Compute all pairwise differences.
  2. Calculate the HSD value.
  3. If the difference between two means exceeds the HSD, they are significantly different.

Bonferroni Method

  • Divide \(\alpha\) by the number of comparisons \(m\).
  • Very conservative for large \(m\).
  • Ensures strong control of FWER.

Scheffé’s Method

  • Applies to any linear contrast, not just pairwise differences.
  • More general but often more conservative (wide confidence intervals).
  • Scheffé’s method is useful when the final set of contrasts is not fully decided a priori.

Balancing Type I Error and Power

Type I Error Control: MCPs reduce the chance of making at least one false claim when testing many hypotheses.

Power Considerations: Strict error control may lower the power to detect real differences. Practitioners must balance the risk of false positives against the risk of missing true effects.

  • Fisher’s LSD: More power, less Type I control.
  • Bonferroni: Strong Type I control, potentially very low power.
  • Tukey’s HSD: Good balance for pairwise comparisons.
  • Scheffé: Very flexible, but can be conservative.

Conclusion

MCPs and contrasts extend the basic ANOVA framework to address realistic experimental questions:

  • MCPs control the “false alarm” rate across multiple inferences.
  • Contrasts provide targeted insight into predetermined scientific hypotheses.
  • Modern statistical software facilitates these procedures, ensuring robust, interpretable results.

Multiple Comparisons and Contrasts - Advanced Materials

When an analysis involves more than one hypothesis test—such as multiple pairwise contrasts after ANOVA or testing multiple outcomes—traditional per-comparison \(\alpha=0.05\) thresholds can lead to an inflated chance of making at least one false discovery. Multiple comparison procedures (MCPs) are designed to ensure the probability of making at least one false rejection (i.e., familywise error rate, FWER) is controlled at a desired level (often 5%).

Overview of Classical Methods

  • Bonferroni Correction
    • Adjustment: \(\alpha^* = \alpha / m\) for each of the \(m\) tests.
    • Confidence Intervals: Expand accordingly by using \(t_{\alpha^*/2,\,df}\) for each test.
    • Advantages: Easy to understand and implement; mathematically guarantees control of the FWER under very general conditions (no need for independence).
    • Disadvantages: Can be overly conservative, especially for large \(m\).
  • Holm’s Step-Down Method
    • An adaptive Bonferroni: Sort p-values from smallest to largest: \(p_{(1)} \le p_{(2)} \le \dots \le p_{(m)}.\)
    • Compare \(p_{(1)}\) to \(\alpha/m\). If significant, compare \(p_{(2)}\) to \(\alpha/(m-1)\), and so on.
    • Advantages: Less conservative than Bonferroni, still guarantees FWER control.
    • Example: If the smallest p-value is less than \(\alpha/m\), reject that hypothesis; then move to the next one with a slightly higher threshold, \(\alpha/(m-1)\). Continue until a test fails to reject or until all tests are done.
  • Hochberg’s Step-Up Method
    • Another adaptive approach, sometimes more powerful than Holm but requires certain assumptions (e.g., independence or some positive dependence structure).
    • Sort p-values in descending order (largest to smallest) and proceed in a step-up fashion.
  • Scheffé’s Method (specific to ANOVA)
    • Designed to test any possible linear contrast among means after a one-way ANOVA.
    • Uses an \(F\)-ratio scaling factor \(\sqrt{(v-1)\,F_{v-1,\,df,\,\alpha}}\) to widen intervals.
    • Pros: Very flexible when many post-hoc contrasts might be considered.
    • Cons: Often considered conservative because it covers the “worst-case” scenario of an infinite number of possible contrasts.
  • Tukey’s Honestly Significant Difference (HSD)
    • Optimized for all pairwise comparisons among \(v\) groups.
    • Relies on the Studentized range statistic \(q\).
    • Typically more powerful than Scheffé when only pairwise comparisons are needed.
  • Dunnett’s Procedure
    • Special-case procedure for comparing several treatments to one control.
    • Uses a multivariate \(t\)-distribution for its critical values, producing narrower intervals (and higher power) than a general method like Bonferroni if this is the only family of tests performed.

Illustration: Pairwise Comparisons After ANOVA

Scenario: You have 4 treatments in a one-way ANOVA, and you want to compare each pair of treatments \((v=4, m=6\) pairwise comparisons). - Bonferroni: Each test at \(\alpha/6\approx 0.0083\). - Tukey’s HSD: R software would provide a critical difference based on the studentized range \(q_{4, df, \alpha}\). This typically yields narrower confidence intervals than Bonferroni because it leverages the specific nature of pairwise testing.

In R:

model <- aov(response ~ treatment, data = your_data)
tukey_result <- TukeyHSD(model, "treatment", conf.level = 0.95)
tukey_result
  • TukeyHSD() automatically applies the Studentized range logic to give adjusted p-values and confidence intervals for each pairwise comparison.

False Discovery Rate (FDR)

For studies with large numbers of hypotheses, such as genomics, proteomics, or large-scale medical analyses, controlling the FWER (i.e., the chance of any false positive) can be very stringent and can drastically reduce statistical power. An alternative is to control the False Discovery Rate (FDR), which is defined as:

\[ \text{FDR} = \mathbb{E}\!\biggl[\frac{\text{Number of False Rejections}}{\text{Total Number of Rejections}}\biggr]. \]

In other words, among all the rejections you make, the FDR method aims to keep the expected proportion of those that are false under a specified level (\(\alpha\)). This is often less conservative than controlling the familywise error rate and can be more suitable when you have hundreds or thousands of tests.

Benjamini–Hochberg (BH) Procedure

Steps:

  • Sort p-values from smallest to largest: \(p_{(1)} \le p_{(2)} \le \cdots \le p_{(m)}\).
  • Find the largest \(k\) such that

\[ p_{(k)} \; \le \; \frac{k}{m}\,\alpha. \]

  • Reject the null hypotheses for all \(p_{(i)}, i \le k\).

  • Interpretation: You keep rejecting from the smallest p-value up to a point where the p-value is no longer below a threshold adjusted by \(\frac{k}{m}\alpha\).

  • Result: On average, the proportion of “rejected hypotheses” that are false positives is capped by \(\alpha\).

Example: Suppose \(m=1000\) tests, with \(\alpha=0.05\). You list p-values in ascending order. If the 40th smallest p-value still satisfies \(p_{(40)} \le (40/1000)*0.05 = 0.002\), you reject that one. But if the 41st smallest p-value is 0.003, which does not meet the criterion \(\le 0.00205\), you stop at 40 rejections.

In R:

p_vals <- c(…)  # your vector of raw p-values
p_adjusted <- p.adjust(p_vals, method = "BH")
# p_adjusted now contains BH-adjusted p-values
significant <- p_adjusted <-  0.05
  • p.adjust(…, method="BH") applies the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure by default.

Other FDR Variants

  • Benjamini–Yekutieli (BY): Accommodates certain dependency structures by replacing \(\frac{k}{m}\alpha\) with \(\frac{k}{m} \frac{\alpha}{\sum_{i=1}^m (1/i)}\). More conservative.
  • Storey’s q-Value approach: Focuses on direct estimation of the proportion of null hypotheses that are true, providing a “q-value” analogous to an FDR-adjusted p-value.

Comparing FDR vs. FWER

Criterion Familywise Error Rate (FWER) False Discovery Rate (FDR)
Definition Probability of at least one false rejection in a set of tests. Expected proportion of false positives among all rejections.
Interpretation Very stringent; zero-tolerance approach: “I want no false positives.” Allows some false positives but controls proportion of errors among significant findings.
Methods Bonferroni, Holm, Hochberg, Tukey, Scheffé, Dunnett, etc. Benjamini–Hochberg, Benjamini–Yekutieli, q-value, etc.
Use Cases Small to moderate number of comparisons (e.g., typical ANOVA-based multiple comparisons). Large-scale testing (e.g., genomics, proteomics, data-mining). More powerful in “big data” scenarios.

Practical Notes and R Guidance

  • Balancing Stringency and Discovery
    • In typical design-of-experiments contexts (small \(m\)), controlling FWER with classical methods (Tukey, Dunnett, Scheffé) is common.
    • In high-dimensional settings (large \(m\)), FDR-based approaches provide more power while accepting a controlled fraction of false positives.
  • Implementation Tips
    • Always report which correction method was used.
    • For one-way ANOVA with moderate group sizes:
      • TukeyHSD for pairwise tests in base R.
      • glht() in multcomp can handle Dunnett’s or other customized contrasts.
    • For large sets of p-values (e.g., from omics or data-mining):
      • Use p.adjust(…, method = "BH") or a specialized package for more advanced FDR control.
  • Be Aware of Underlying Assumptions
    • Some methods (e.g., Hochberg’s step-up) assume independence or positive correlation among tests.
    • If dependencies are complex, it can alter the conservativeness of the corrections.

Extended Example

High-Throughput Screening: Suppose you test 1000 compounds for their effect on tumor cell viability. You collect p-values for each compound’s effect vs. control. Without any correction:

\[ \text{FWER} = 1 - (1-0.05)^{1000} \approx 1 \; (\text{virtually } 100\%\text{ chance of at least one false positive}). \]

  • FWER Control (Bonferroni): \(\alpha^* = 0.05 / 1000 = 0.00005.\) This drastically reduces your chance of even a single false discovery, but many true effects could be missed.
  • FDR Control (Benjamini–Hochberg): Typically yields a threshold that might let you reject dozens or hundreds of null hypotheses while controlling the expected proportion of false positives among your rejections at 5%.

In R:

# Suppose p_raw is a vector of 1000 p-values from the tests
p_vals_adj_bonf <- p.adjust(p_raw, method = "bonferroni")
p_vals_adj_bh <- p.adjust(p_raw, method = "BH")

# Summaries:
sum(p_vals_adj_bonf < 0.05)  # how many are called significant under Bonferroni
sum(p_vals_adj_bh < 0.05)    # how many are called significant under BH
  • Expect the Bonferroni set of significant tests to be a subset of the BH set.
  • The BH procedure, by allowing for a controlled fraction of false positives, typically identifies more “discoveries.”

Conclusion

Both familywise error rate (FWER) and false discovery rate (FDR) control are essential concepts for valid statistical inference in the face of multiple comparisons:

  • FWER methods (like Bonferroni, Holm, Tukey, Scheffé, Dunnett) ensure that the probability of any false positive across all tests remains below \(\alpha\).
  • FDR methods (like Benjamini–Hochberg) allow some false positives but control their proportion among all discoveries, making them particularly valuable for large-scale testing contexts.

Understanding these frameworks—and carefully choosing which to apply—helps maintain the integrity of your findings while balancing the risk of missing real effects.

Appendix: Advanced Mathematical Details

Proofs and Derivations

Variance of a Contrast

For a contrast \(L = \sum c_i \mu_i\), assuming independent samples of size \(n_i\) and variance \(\sigma^2\):

\[ \hat{L} = \sum c_i \bar{Y}_i, \quad \text{Var}(\hat{L}) = \sigma^2 \sum \frac{c_i^2}{n_i}. \]

Proof involves linearity of expectation and variance properties under independence and homoscedasticity (Dean et al., 2017).

Derivation of Tukey’s HSD

Tukey’s HSD critical value is derived from the studentized range distribution \(q\):

  1. Let \(\text{max diff} = \max_i(\bar{Y}_i) - \min_i(\bar{Y}_i)\).
  2. Under \(H_0\), \(\frac{\text{max diff}}{\sqrt{\text{MSE}/n}}\) follows a studentized range distribution.
  3. Invert this to find the critical range \(q_{\alpha; v,n-v}\).

(Montgomery, 2020)

Scheffé’s Method

Scheffé’s intervals are based on the fact that any linear contrast of treatment means can be bounded using a \((v-1)F\)-distribution multiplier:

\[ L \in \hat{L} \pm \sqrt{(v-1)F_{\alpha; v-1, n-v}}\cdot \text{SE}(\hat{L}). \]

The proof uses the union-intersection principle and geometry of the parameter space (Christensen, 2018).
