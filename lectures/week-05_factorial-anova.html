Objectives

  1. Understand the structure and terminology of two-factor factorial designs.
  2. Distinguish between main effects and interaction effects in a two-way ANOVA.
  3. Conduct a two-way ANOVA, interpret results, and create interaction plots in R.
  4. Handle complexities such as unbalanced designs and understand different types of sums of squares.

Checking the Assumptions on the Model

The assumptions for both the two-way complete model and the two-way main-effects model are that the error random variables have equal variances, are mutually independent, and are normally distributed. The strategy for checking these assumptions follows the procedures outlined in Chapter 5 (see Dean et al. 2017).

Standardized Residuals

The standardized residuals are computed as

\[ z_{ijt} = \frac{y_{ijt} - \hat{y}_{ijt}}{\sqrt{SS_{Error}/(n - 1)}}, \]

where

  • \(\hat{y}_{ijt} = \hat{\tau}_{ij} = \hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_j + (\alpha\beta)_{ij}\) (two-way complete model), or
  • \(\hat{y}_{ijt} = \hat{\tau}_{ij} = \hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_j\) (two-way main-effects model),

depending on which model is used. A “hat” denotes a least squares estimate.

Typical residual plots to check:

  • Observation Order vs. Residuals to assess independence.
  • Levels of Each Factor (and possibly \(\hat{y}_{ijt}\)) vs. Residuals to detect outliers and variance inhomogeneity.
  • Normal Q-Q Plot (Normal Scores) to assess normality.

When using the main-effects model, interaction plots (e.g., Figures 6.1, 6.2 in reference texts) help visualize any potential interaction. Another method is to plot standardized residuals against one factor’s levels while labeling or grouping by the second factor’s levels.

All about Interaction

Concept of Interaction

Definition: An interaction occurs when the effect of one factor depends on the level of the other factor.

  • In literature, Fisher (1926) emphasized that interaction reveals non-additivity among factors. Dean et al. (2017) define interaction as “the failure of factors to combine additively in their effects on the response.”
  • Graphical perspective: If lines in an interaction plot are not parallel, an interaction is likely present.

Why Interaction Matters

  • If significant, you cannot interpret main effects alone.
  • Must interpret or compare the cell means \(\mu_{ij}\) directly.

Scenario: Effect of Diet and Exercise on Weight Loss

Suppose a researcher wants to study the impact of diet type and exercise routine on weight loss. The two factors are:

  • Factor A: Diet Type
    • \(A_1\): Low-Carb Diet
    • \(A_2\): Low-Fat Diet
  • Factor B: Exercise Routine
    • \(B_1\): Cardio Exercise
    • \(B_2\): Strength Training

The response variable is weight loss (kg) after 3 months.

Case 1: No Interaction (Additive Effects)

If the effects of diet and exercise are purely additive, we might see the following mean weight loss data:

Exercise Type Low-Carb Diet (\(A_1\)) Low-Fat Diet (\(A_2\)) Effect of Diet
Cardio (\(B_1\)) 8 kg 5 kg 3 kg
Strength Training (\(B_2\)) 6 kg 3 kg 3 kg
Effect of Exercise 2 kg 2 kg

Key Observations

  • Main Effect of Diet: Low-carb results in 3 kg more weight loss than low-fat on average (marginal means: \((8+6)/2 = 7\) vs. \((5+3)/2 = 4\)).
  • Main Effect of Exercise: Cardio results in 2 kg more weight loss than strength training on average (marginal means: \((8+5)/2 = 6.5\) vs. \((6+3)/2 = 4.5\)).
  • No Interaction: The effect of diet is consistent across exercise types (difference = 2 kg for both), and the effect of exercise is consistent across diets (difference = 3 kg for both).

Case 2: Significant Interaction (Non-Additive Effects)

If diet and exercise interact, the results may look different:

Exercise Type Low-Carb Diet (\(A_1\)) Low-Fat Diet (\(A_2\)) Effect of Exercise
Cardio (\(B_1\)) 10 kg 3 kg 7 kg
Strength Training (\(B_2\)) 4 kg 5 kg -1 kg
Effect of Diet 6 kg -2 kg

Key Observations:

  • On a Low-Carb Diet, Cardio leads to the highest weight loss (10 kg), while Strength Training leads to much less weight loss (4 kg).
  • On a Low-Fat Diet, Cardio is less effective (3 kg), and Strength Training is slightly better (5 kg).
  • Interaction Effect: The impact of exercise type depends on the diet.
    • Cardio is very effective with a Low-Carb Diet (+10 kg loss) but ineffective with a Low-Fat Diet (only 3 kg loss).
    • Strength Training performs better with Low-Fat than Low-Carb Diet.
    • The simple main effects of diet and exercise change direction in different conditions.

How Interaction Changes Interpretation

  1. Without Interaction (Additive Model):
    • The best strategy would be Low-Carb Diet and Cardio, assuming effects are independent.
    • Strength Training would be equally beneficial across diets.
  2. With Interaction (Non-Additive Model):
    • Cardio only works well with a Low-Carb Diet.
    • Strength Training works better with a Low-Fat Diet.
    • Recommending Cardio to someone on a Low-Fat Diet might be counterproductive.
    • If a person prefers Strength Training, they might be better off on a Low-Fat Diet rather than a Low-Carb Diet.

Graphical Representation

If we plot weight loss against exercise type, the interaction would appear as non-parallel lines:

  • No interaction: Two parallel lines (consistent gap between exercise types).
  • Strong interaction: Lines cross or diverge significantly, indicating a change in ranking of factors.
# Create data frames for both scenarios

no_interaction <- data.frame(
    Diet = rep(c("Low-Carb", "Low-Fat"), each = 2),
    Exercise = rep(c("Cardio", "Strength"), times = 2),
    Weight_Loss = c(8, 6, 5, 3)
)

with_interaction <- data.frame(
    Diet = rep(c("Low-Carb", "Low-Fat"), each = 2),
    Exercise = rep(c("Cardio", "Strength"), times = 2),
    Weight_Loss = c(10, 4, 3, 5)
)

# Plot 1: No Interaction
plot1 <- ggplot(no_interaction, aes(x = Diet, y = Weight_Loss, color = Exercise, group = Exercise)) +
    geom_line() +
    geom_point(size = 3) +
    labs(y = "Weight Loss (kg)", title = "No Interaction")

# Plot 2: With Interaction
plot2 <- ggplot(with_interaction, aes(x = Diet, y = Weight_Loss, color = Exercise, group = Exercise)) +

    geom_point(size = 3) +

Interpretation and Conclusion

This example shows how interaction effects significantly impact decision-making. Ignoring interactions could lead to misleading conclusions:

  • A “best diet” recommendation without considering exercise type would be incorrect.
  • A “best exercise” recommendation without considering diet type would be misleading.

Thus, interaction terms are crucial in Two-Factor ANOVA to uncover hidden dependencies between factors and provide accurate interpretations of experimental results.

Interpreting The Significant Interaction Effect in Two-Factor ANOVA

Since the interaction effect (\(p = 0.0156\)) is statistically significant (\(p < 0.05\)), we do not interpret the main effects of Battery Type and Brand separately. Instead, we focus on the interaction, which means that the effect of one factor depends on the level of the other factor.

Interaction Effect Interpretation

Interaction (BatteryType:Brand, \(F\) = 9.356, \(p\) = 0.0156)

  • The significant interaction suggests that the impact of Battery Type on Battery Life is different for different Brands.
  • In other words, the difference between Alkaline and Heavy Duty batteries varies depending on whether the battery is a Name Brand or a Store Brand.
  • Because the interaction term is significant, we must analyze the individual cell means rather than summarizing the main effects.

Cell Means (Group Means by Factor Levels)

To understand the interaction, we analyze the mean Battery Life for each combination of Battery Type and Brand.

Battery Type Brand Mean Battery Life (\(\bar{Y}_{ij}\))
Alkaline Name Brand \(\bar{Y}_{11} = 32.0\)
Alkaline Store Brand \(\bar{Y}_{12} = 28.7\)
Heavy Duty Name Brand \(\bar{Y}_{21} = 25.7\)
Heavy Duty Store Brand \(\bar{Y}_{22} = 20.7\)

Key Observations from the Cell Means

  • Alkaline batteries last longer than Heavy Duty batteries within each brand, but the size of this effect is not the same for both brands.
  • Name Brand batteries consistently outperform Store Brand batteries, but the gap is larger for Alkaline batteries than for Heavy Duty batteries.
  • The difference between Alkaline and Heavy Duty is larger for Store Brand (8 hours) than for Name Brand (6.3 hours).
  • This suggests a non-additive effect, where the difference between battery types depends on the brand.

Calculating the Interaction Effect

Mathematically, the interaction effect can be computed as:

\[ \text{Interaction Effect} = (\bar{Y}_{11} - \bar{Y}_{12}) - (\bar{Y}_{21} - \bar{Y}_{22}) \] \[ = (32.0 - 28.7) - (25.7 - 20.7) \] \[ = 3.3 - 5.0 \] \[ = -1.7 \]

What This Means:

  • The difference between Alkaline and Heavy Duty batteries is not the same across brands.
  • The gap is larger for Store Brand batteries than for Name Brand batteries.
  • The negative interaction effect (-1.7) means that the effect of Battery Type is slightly weaker for Name Brand batteries compared to Store Brand batteries.

Graphical Representation

A graphical interaction plot** helps visualize the effect:

interaction.plot(
    x.factor     = battery_data$BatteryType,
    trace.factor = battery_data$Brand,
    response     = battery_data$Life,
    type         = "b",
    pch          = c(1, 19),
    col          = c("blue", "red"),
    xlab         = "Battery Type",
    ylab         = "Mean Battery Life",
    trace.label  = "Brand"
)
  • If lines cross or deviate from parallel, that supports the significant interaction.

Expected Patterns:

  • Parallel Lines → No interaction (the effect of one factor is consistent across levels of the other).
  • Non-Parallel Lines → Interaction present (the effect of one factor changes based on the other).
  • Lines Crossing → Strong interaction (e.g., one factor reverses the effect of the other).

Since the interaction is significant, we expect the lines to be non-parallel, indicating that the difference in battery life between Alkaline and Heavy Duty depends on the brand.

Contrasts

Contrasts for Main Effects and Interactions

Recalling the cell-means model

\[ y_{ijt} = \mu + \tau_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ijt}, \]

equivalent to a one-way ANOVA model with \(ab\) treatments, we have that all contrasts in \(\tau_{ij}\) are estimable. In two-way layouts, common contrasts include:

  1. Treatment Contrasts \(\sum_{i,j} d_{ij}\,\tau_{ij}\).
  2. Interaction Contrasts measure non-parallelism in interaction plots. For instance,

\[ (\tau_{sh} - \tau_{(s+1)h}) - (\tau_{sq} - \tau_{(s+1)q}). \]

Using the two-way complete model \(\tau_{ij} = \alpha_i + \beta_j + (\alpha\beta)_{ij}\), an interaction contrast zeroes out if \((\alpha\beta)_{ij}=0\) for all \(i,j\).

Writing Contrasts as Coefficient Lists

When working in the two-way complete model, we can express contrasts in two ways:

  • As a list of coefficients of \(\alpha_i^*\), \(\beta_j^*\), and \((\alpha\beta)_{ij}\).
  • As a list of coefficients of the \(\tau_{ij}\).

Example 6.3.1 (Battery Experiment) A two-factor experiment with two levels each: “duty” (1 = alkaline, 2 = heavy duty) and “brand” (1 = name brand, 2 = store brand). Treatment combinations are \((1,1)\), \((1,2)\), \((2,1)\), and \((2,2)\), each with \(r=4\) observations. An interaction contrast might be

\[ \tau_{11} - \tau_{12} - \tau_{21} + \tau_{22} \;=\; (\alpha\beta)_{11} - (\alpha\beta)_{12} - (\alpha\beta)_{21} + (\alpha\beta)_{22}. \]

Analysis of the Two-Way Complete Model

When two factors may interact, we often begin with:

  1. Two-Way Complete Model (also called the full cell-means model),

\[ y_{ijt} = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_j + (\alpha\beta)_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ijt}, \quad \varepsilon_{ijt}\sim \text{N}(0,\sigma^2). \]

  1. Hypothesis Test for No Interaction. If there is insufficient evidence of interaction, we proceed to examine main effects under the same complete model. If interaction is significant, we return to the cell-means perspective to compare treatment combinations directly.

Least Squares Estimators

From standard ANOVA theory,

  • \(\hat{\mu} + \hat{\tau}_{ij} = \hat{\mu} + \hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_j + \widehat{(\alpha\beta)}_{ij} = \bar{Y}_{ij\cdot}\),
  • \(\text{Var}(\bar{Y}_{ij\cdot}) = \sigma^2 / r_{ij}\).

An interaction contrast \(\sum_{i,j} d_{ij}\,\tau_{ij}\) has LSE \(\sum_{i,j} d_{ij}\,\bar{Y}_{ij\cdot}\) with variance \(\sigma^2 \sum d_{ij}^2/r_{ij}\).

Estimation of \(\sigma^2\)

Because the two-way complete model is equivalent to a one-way ANOVA with \(ab\) cells, an unbiased estimate of \(\sigma^2\) is obtained via the error sum of squares:

\[ SS_{Error} = \sum_{i,j,t} (y_{ijt} - \bar{Y}_{ij\cdot})^2, \]

with \(v = ab\) levels and \(n = \sum_{i,j} r_{ij}\) total observations. The corresponding mean square error is

\[ MS_{Error} = \frac{SS_{Error}}{n - v}. \]

Example 6.4.2 (Reaction Time Experiment) A pilot experiment with \(a=2\) levels of “Cue Stimulus” and \(b=3\) levels of “Cue Time,” each having \(r=3\) replications, yields data in Table 6.2. The sum of squares for error is computed as

\[ SS_{Error} = \sum_{i,j,t} y_{ijt}^2 \;-\; \sum_{i,j} r_{ij}\,\bar{Y}_{ij\cdot}^2 \;=\; 0.00347, \]

and hence \(MS_{Error} = SS_{Error}/(n-ab)\).

Multiple Comparisons with Unequal Variances

If variance heterogeneity persists and cannot be corrected by transformations, one can use Satterthwaite’s approximation (Christensen 2018).

Analysis of Variance for the Complete Model

We commonly examine three hypotheses:

  1. Interaction \(H_{0}^{AB}\): \((\alpha\beta)_{ij} = 0\) for all \(i,j\).
  2. Main Effect of A \(H_{0}^{A}\): \(\alpha_1^* = \alpha_2^* = \cdots = \alpha_a^*\).
  3. Main Effect of B \(H_{0}^{B}\): \(\beta_1^* = \beta_2^* = \cdots = \beta_b^*\).

If \(H_{0}^{AB}\) is not rejected, we then focus on main effects. Otherwise, the cell-means model is typically used to compare all \(ab\) treatment combinations.

Example 6.4.5 (Reaction Time Experiment, continued) An ANOVA table yields:

  • \(MS_{AB}/MS_{Error} = 1.46 < F_{2,12,0.01} = 6.93\) (Fail to reject interaction)
  • \(MS_{A}/MS_{Error} = 81.38 > 9.33\) (Reject \(H_{0}^{A}\))
  • \(MS_{B}/MS_{Error} = 2.00 < 6.93\) (Fail to reject \(H_{0}^{B}\))

Analysis of the Two-Way Main-Effects Model

When the interaction is deemed negligible (or theoretically disallowed), the two-way main-effects model is:

\[ y_{ijt} = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_j + \varepsilon_{ijt}, \quad \varepsilon_{ijt} \sim \text{N}(0,\sigma^2). \]

Least Squares Estimators (Equal \(r\))

If \(r\) is constant across all \(ab\) cells, the LSE of \(E[y_{ijt}] = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_j\) is

\[ \hat{\mu} + \hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_j = \bar{Y}_{i\cdot\cdot} + \bar{Y}_{\cdot j \cdot} - \bar{Y}_{\cdot\cdot\cdot}. \]

Pairwise comparisons (e.g., \(\alpha_p - \alpha_s\)) become \(\bar{Y}_{p\cdot\cdot} - \bar{Y}_{s\cdot\cdot}\).

Example 6.5.1 (Nail Varnish Experiment) Two solvents (\(A\)) and three brands of varnish (\(B\)), each with \(r=5\). From the data, the difference in solvents is estimated as

\[ \hat{\alpha}_1 - \hat{\alpha}_2 = \bar{Y}_{1\cdot\cdot} - \bar{Y}_{2\cdot\cdot} \approx -3.6040. \]

Estimation of \(\sigma^2\) (Main-Effects Model)

With \(n = abr\), the sum of squares of errors is

\[ SS_{Error} = \sum_{i=1}^a \sum_{j=1}^b \sum_{t=1}^{r} \left( y_{ijt} - \bar{Y}_{i\cdot\cdot} - \bar{Y}_{\cdot j \cdot} + \bar{Y}_{\cdot\cdot\cdot}\right)^2. \]

Then

\[ MS_{Error} = \frac{SS_{Error}}{n - a - b + 1}. \]

Example 6.5.2 (Nail Varnish Experiment, continued) With \(a=2\), \(b=3\), \(r=5\), the sum of squares \(SS_{Error}=216.7762\) and \(MS_{Error} = 216.7762/(30-2-3+1) \approx 8.3375\).

Multiple Comparisons in the Main-Effects Model

For equal sample sizes, Tukey, Bonferroni, and Scheffé methods still apply to main-effect contrasts. For example, a set of 100\((1-\alpha)\%\) simultaneous CIs for factor-A contrasts has the form:

\[ \sum_i c_i \alpha_i \;\in\; \sum_i c_i \bar{Y}_{i\cdot\cdot} \;\pm\; w \; \sqrt{MS_{Error} \,\sum_i \frac{c_i^2}{br}}, \]

where \(w\) depends on the chosen procedure (Dean et al. 2017).

Example 6.5.3 (Nail Varnish Experiment, continued) Simultaneous confidence intervals for differences among three varnishes can be constructed via Tukey’s HSD with \(q_{3,26,0.01} = 4.54\).

Analysis of Variance for Equal Sample Sizes

When \(r\) is constant:

  • \(H_{0}^B: \{\beta_1 = \beta_2 = \dots = \beta_b\}\) is tested via

\[ \text{reject if}\;\; \frac{MS_{B}}{MS_{Error}} > F_{b-1,\,n-a-b+1,\,\alpha}. \]

  • \(H_{0}^A: \{\alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = \dots = \alpha_a\}\) analogously.

Example 6.5.4 (Nail Varnish Experiment) An ANOVA table yields \(F\)-ratios for factor \(A\) (solvent) and factor \(B\) (varnish). Solvent is significant while varnish is not.

Calculating Sample Sizes

Both the power-based approach (Sections 3.6) and confidence-interval-width approach (Sections 4.5) can be extended to two-way designs (Montgomery 2020). For main-effect differences of size \(A\) or \(B\), formulas such as

\[ r = \frac{2a\,\sigma^2\,\phi^2}{b\,A^2}, \quad r = \frac{2b\,\sigma^2\,\phi^2}{a\,B^2} \]

may be used to assure adequate power, where \(\phi\) is a function of the noncentral \(F\)-distribution (see also Christensen 2018).

Small Experiments

One Observation Per Cell

When \(r=1\) per cell, if an interaction is possible, we cannot estimate \(\sigma^2\) directly (because \(\text{df for error} = ab(r-1) = 0\)). The study design must:

  • Assume interaction is negligible (use main-effects model), or
  • Have \(\sigma^2\) known in advance, or
  • Model only a reduced set of contrasts (sparsity of effects).

Analysis Based on Orthogonal Contrasts

Two contrasts \(c_i\tau_i\) and \(k_i\tau_i\) are orthogonal if

\[ \sum_i \frac{c_i\,k_i}{r_i} = 0. \]

A complete set of \(v-1\) orthogonal contrasts partitions the treatment sum of squares:

\[ SS_{Total} = \sum_{q=1}^{v-1} SS_{c_q}. \]

Example 6.7.1 (Battery Experiment, continued) Duty, brand, and interaction contrasts form a complete set of \(3\) orthogonal contrasts for \(4\) total treatments, and their sums of squares add to the total.

Tukey’s Test for Additivity

When the interaction is thought to be of the form \((\alpha\beta)_{ij} = \gamma\,\alpha_i\,\beta_j\), Tukey’s test uses only 1 df. The decision rule is

\[ \text{reject } H_0^\gamma \text{ if } \frac{ss_{AB}^*}{SS_{Error}/e} > F_{1,e,\alpha}, \]

where \(ss_{AB}^*\) is a computed sum of squares specifically for that parametric form of interaction.

A Real Experiment—Air Velocity Experiment

Table 6.11 shows data on air velocity for three rib heights (\(A\)) and six Reynolds numbers (\(B\)) with \(r=1\) per cell. Investigators suspected some interaction but believed certain higher-order trends would be negligible. They pooled those negligible interaction contrasts for an error estimate with 3 df, then tested the remaining contrasts individually. See Table 6.12 for the resulting ANOVA.

Model Checking and Residual Diagnostics

Before finalizing an ANOVA conclusion, we must check:

  • Independence: Plot residuals vs. observation order.
  • Constant Variance: Plot residuals (or standardized residuals) vs. fitted values, factor levels, or treatment combos.
  • Normality: Use a Normal Q-Q plot of residuals.

The standardized residual often used is:

\[ z_{ijt} = \frac{y_{ijt} - \hat{y}_{ijt}}{\sqrt{\tfrac{SS_{Error}}{n - 1}}}. \]

Depending on whether we assume a complete or main-effects model, \(\hat{y}_{ijt}\) includes:

  • \(\hat{\tau}_{ij} = \hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_j + \widehat{(\alpha\beta)}_{ij}\) (complete model), or
  • \(\hat{\tau}_{ij} = \hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_j\) (main-effects model).

Two-Way ANOVA Models in R

This section demonstrates how to fit and interpret two-way ANOVA models in R, focusing on attached code examples. We assume a data frame such as react.data containing:

  • A, B: numerical or factor-coded levels of Factors A and B,
  • y: the response,
  • potentially additional identifying information (observation order, treatment codes, etc.).

Fitting the Two-Way Complete Model

A typical R formula for the two-way complete model is:

modelAB = aov(y ~ fA + fB + fA:fB, data = react_df)

or equivalently:

modelAB = aov(y ~ fA * fB, data = react_df)

Here, fA and fB are factor variables in R. The operator * adds both main effects and the interaction term. We often check:

Balanced Vs. Unbalanced Data: Comprehensive Guide

Intuitive Understanding

In statistical modeling, especially in Analysis of Variance (ANOVA), the concepts of balanced and unbalanced designs are fundamental:

  • Balanced Design: Each factor-level combination has the same number of observations, making calculations straightforward and allowing different sums of squares methods (Type I, II, III) to yield the same results.
  • Unbalanced Design: Some factor levels have more observations than others, complicating interpretations and requiring careful choice of sums of squares to ensure valid comparisons.

Why does balance matter?

  • In a balanced design, main effects and interaction effects are easier to interpret because all levels contribute equally to variance.
  • In an unbalanced design, estimates depend on the order of factor entry in the model, leading to different results based on the sum of squares method used.

Mathematical Foundations of Sums of Squares (SS)

1. Type I (Sequential) Sums of Squares

  • Type I SS calculates the sum of squares sequentially based on the order in which factors are entered into the model.
  • Each factor’s effect is tested before adjusting for other factors.
  • The order of entry matters, meaning later factors are tested after accounting for earlier ones.

Mathematical Formulation

For a model with two factors, \(A\) and \(B\), and their interaction:

\[ SS_{\text{Type I}} = SS(A) + SS(B \mid A) + SS(A \times B \mid A, B) \]

where:

  • \(SS(A)\) tests the effect of A alone.
  • \(SS(B \mid A)\) tests B after accounting for A.
  • \(SS(A \times B \mid A, B)\) tests the interaction after both main effects.

When To Use Type I SS?

  • For hierarchical models where the order of factor entry reflects natural causation.
  • For regression models where variables are added in a meaningful sequence.
  • For unbalanced data, use with caution—it is biased toward factors entered earlier.

2. Type II (Adjusted) Sums of Squares

  • Type II SS tests each main effect after accounting for all other main effects but without interactions.
  • Assumes no interaction between factors when computing main effects.

Mathematical Formulation

\[ SS_{\text{Type II}} = SS(A \mid B) + SS(B \mid A) + SS(A \times B \mid A, B) \]

where:

  • \(SS(A \mid B)\) tests A after controlling for B but ignores the interaction.
  • \(SS(B \mid A)\) tests B similarly.
  • The interaction term is tested last.

When To Use Type II SS?

  • When interactions are not significant and main effects are of primary interest.
  • For regression models where predictors are independent.
  • For unbalanced designs, when interactions are weak.

3. Type III (Fully Adjusted) Sums of Squares

  • Type III SS adjusts for all other terms in the model, including interactions.
  • It tests each factor as if it were the last entered, making it the most interpretable for unbalanced designs.

Mathematical Formulation

\[ SS_{\text{Type III}} = SS(A \mid B, A \times B) + SS(B \mid A, A \times B) + SS(A \times B \mid A, B) \]

where:

  • Each term is tested after controlling for all other effects, including interactions.

When To Use Type III SS?

  • For unbalanced designs where fair comparisons between factors are needed.
  • For factorial ANOVA models when interactions are present.
  • For categorical variables, especially with contrasts like contr.sum.

R Implementation: Computing Type III SS Using car::Anova()

To obtain Type III SS in R, we must ensure the correct contrasts are set:

# Set contrast options for Type III SS
options(contrasts = c("contr.sum", "contr.poly"))

# Fit the ANOVA model
model <- lm(Response ~ FactorA * FactorB, data = dataset)

# Compute Type III SS using car::Anova()
library(car)
Anova(model, type = "III")

Alternative Approach: drop1() for Type III SS

Another way to obtain Type III SS in R is:

drop1(model, ~., test = "F")  # Ensures Type III if contrasts are set correctly

This method is equivalent to car::Anova() but requires contrast settings to be explicitly defined beforehand.

One Observation per Cell (Small Experiments)

When r=1r=1 per cell, there is no direct error estimate under the full two-way complete model—the error df is zero. One can:

  1. Assume interaction is negligible (use main-effects model only),
  2. Know \(\sigma^2\) a priori,
  3. Use orthogonal contrasts to identify “negligible” components of the interaction, thereby pooling them to form an error estimate.

Advanced Topics

Orthogonal Contrasts

Two contrasts are orthogonal if their corresponding sums of squares are uncorrelated. Equivalently, for equal \(r_{ij}=r\), the sum of products of coefficients must be zero.

Model Building and Type I SS

For building or refining a model, Type I SS can be used to see how adding each effect in sequence explains residual variation.
