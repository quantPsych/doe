Week 1: Introduction to Experimental Design

Topics Covered

  • Purpose and scope of experimental design
  • Observational studies vs. designed experiments
  • Key principles: randomization, replication, and control

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate between observational studies and experiments.
  • Explain the three fundamental principles of experimental design.
  • Recognize scenarios where designed experiments are beneficial.

Class Activities

  • Lecture: Introduction to experimentation (Dean et al., Ch. 1, Sec. 1.1–1.1.1; Ch. 2, Sec. 2.1–2.2).
  • Case Study Discussion: The role of experimental design in manufacturing and biomedical research.
  • Hands-on Activity: Simple randomization exercises in R.

Homework

  • Homework 1: Basics of Experimental Design (due Feb. 6).

Week 2: Completely Randomized Designs (CRD)

Topics Covered

  • Structure and application of CRD
  • One-way ANOVA model and assumptions
  • Conducting and interpreting ANOVA in R

Learning Objectives

  • Design and analyze experiments using CRD.
  • Conduct one-way ANOVA and interpret results.
  • Check ANOVA assumptions and assess validity.

Class Activities

  • Lecture: CRD framework and implementation (Dean et al., Ch. 3, Sec. 3.1–3.5).
  • In-class Example: One-way ANOVA in R using real-world data.
  • Group Discussion: Interpreting ANOVA tables and visualizing results.

Homework

  • Homework 2: CRD Analysis (due Feb. 13).

Week 3: Multiple Comparisons

Topics Covered

  • Post-hoc tests: Tukey, Bonferroni, Scheffé
  • Contrasts: linear, orthogonal, and non-orthogonal comparisons

Learning Objectives

  • Implement multiple comparison methods in R.
  • Interpret post-hoc test results.
  • Apply linear contrasts for deeper treatment comparisons.

Class Activities

  • Lecture: Multiple comparisons and contrasts (Dean et al., Ch. 4, Sec. 4.1–4.4).
  • R Demonstration: Tukey’s HSD, Bonferroni correction, and Scheffé method.
  • Practice Exercise: Identifying significant treatment differences.

Homework

  • Homework 3: Multiple Comparisons (due Feb. 20).

Week 4: Checking Model Assumptions

Topics Covered

  • Residual diagnostics
  • Detecting non-normality and heteroscedasticity
  • Applying transformations

Learning Objectives

  • Conduct residual analysis to check ANOVA assumptions.
  • Implement transformations when needed.
  • Identify and handle outliers in experimental data.

Class Activities

  • Lecture: Model assumption checking (Dean et al., Ch. 5, Sec. 5.1–5.3).
  • R Practical: Visualizing residuals and testing normality.
  • Group Activity: Analyzing transformed data.

Homework

  • Homework 4: Residual Analysis (due Feb. 27).

Week 5: Two-Way Factorial Designs

Topics Covered

  • Two-factor factorial structure
  • Main effects and interactions
  • Two-way ANOVA model and interpretation

Learning Objectives

  • Implement and analyze two-way factorial designs.
  • Interpret interaction plots and ANOVA tables.
  • Recognize the significance of main effects vs. interactions.

Class Activities

  • Lecture: Factorial design theory (Dean et al., Ch. 6, Sec. 6.1–6.5).
  • R Practical: Running and interpreting a two-way ANOVA.
  • Class Discussion: Interaction effects and their implications.

Homework

  • Homework 5: Two-Factor ANOVA (due Mar. 6).

Week 6: Higher-Order Factorial Designs

Topics Covered

  • Three-way factorial designs
  • Higher-order interactions
  • Managing unbalanced designs

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze three-factor experiments.
  • Identify and interpret complex interactions.
  • Handle missing or unbalanced data in factorial designs.

Class Activities

  • Lecture: Higher-order factorial designs (Dean et al., Ch. 7, Sec. 7.1–7.6).
  • R Demonstration: Analyzing a three-way factorial experiment.
  • Discussion: Real-world applications of high-order designs.

Homework

  • Homework 6: Higher-Order Factorial Analysis (due Mar. 6).

Week 7: Midterm Review and Exam

  • Midterm Review Session: Key concepts from Weeks 1–6.
  • Midterm Exam: Covers CRD, factorial designs, and multiple comparisons.

Week 8: Blocking & Randomized Complete Block Design (RCBD)

Topics Covered

  • Concept of blocking
  • RCBD structure and analysis
  • Efficiency of blocking designs

Learning Objectives

  • Apply RCBD principles to experimental design.
  • Conduct ANOVA for blocked designs.
  • Assess the efficiency of blocking.

Class Activities

  • Lecture: RCBD and blocking efficiency (Dean et al., Ch. 10, Sec. 10.1–10.4).
  • R Demonstration: Implementing RCBD.
  • Discussion: Comparing blocking to CRD.

Homework

  • Homework 7: RCBD Analysis (due Mar. 25).

Week 9: Spring Break – No Classes

Week 10: Complete Block Designs

Topics Covered

  • Extensions of RCBD
  • Factorial treatments within blocks
  • Variance components analysis

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze complete block designs.
  • Estimate variance components.
  • Compare efficiency of different blocking approaches.

Class Activities

  • Lecture: Advanced blocking designs (Dean et al., Ch. 10, Sec. 10.6–10.8).
  • R Practical: Implementing factorial treatments in blocks.
  • Group Activity: Evaluating blocking efficiency.

Homework

  • Homework 8: Complete Block Designs (due Apr. 3).

Week 11: Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA)

Topics Covered

  • Incorporating covariates into ANOVA
  • Adjusted treatment means
  • ANCOVA assumptions

Learning Objectives

  • Implement ANCOVA in R.
  • Interpret adjusted means and significance.
  • Apply ANCOVA in experimental studies.

Class Activities

  • Lecture: ANCOVA theory and application (Dean et al., Ch. 9, Sec. 9.1–9.4).
  • R Practical: Performing ANCOVA and interpreting results.
  • Discussion: Comparing ANCOVA to standard ANOVA.

Homework

  • Homework 9: ANCOVA Analysis (due Apr. 10).

Week 12–15: Advanced Experimental Designs

  • Row–Column Designs (Latin Squares) (Week 12)
  • Random & Mixed Models (Week 13)
  • Nested Models (Week 14)
  • Split-Plot Designs (Week 15)

Each week will include: - Lectures on theoretical foundations. - R demonstrations on implementing models. - Group discussions on real-world applications.

Homework: - Week 12: Row–Column Analysis (due Apr. 17). - Week 13: Random and Mixed Models (due Apr. 24). - Week 14: Nested Models (due May 1). - Week 15: Split-Plot Analysis (due May 6).

Week 16: Catching Up and Review

  • Review key concepts from Weeks 1–15.
  • Address common misconceptions and questions.
  • Provide additional practice problems.

Week 17: Final Exam

  • Date: Tuesday, May 13, 2025.
  • Content: Comprehensive (Weeks 1–15).
  • Format: Short-answer, computations, and application-based problems.
