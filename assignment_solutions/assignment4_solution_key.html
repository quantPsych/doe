(a) Check the Equal-Variance Assumption

  1. Data Overview

    • Four treatments (three margarine brands labeled 1, 2, 3, and one butter brand labeled 4).
    • Sample sizes: All four groups have \(n_i = 10\).
    • Sample means \(\bar{Y}_i\):

\[ \bar{Y}_1 = 176.4,\quad \bar{Y}_2 = 238.6,\quad \bar{Y}_3 = 171.4,\quad \bar{Y}_4 = 208.9 \]

  • Sample standard deviations \(s_i\):

\[ s_1 = 5.56,\quad s_2 = 8.66,\quad s_3 = 4.27,\quad s_4 = 8.45 \]

  1. Visual/Descriptive Checks

    • Compare largest \(s_i\) to smallest \(s_i\): \(\max(s_i) = 8.66\) (Brand 2) and \(\min(s_i) = 4.27\) (Brand 3).
      • The ratio \(\frac{8.66}{4.27} \approx 2.03\).
    • A quick rule of thumb is that a ratio above 2 or 3 suggests potential violations of homoscedasticity.

  2. Formal Test (Optional Illustrative Approaches)

    • Levene’s Test or Bartlett’s Test can be used:
      • Typically, a small \(p\)-value (e.g., \(p < 0.05\)) indicates variance heterogeneity.
    • Suppose the test indicates we reject \(H_0\) of equal variances.

(a) Check the Equal-Variance Assumption

Answer

  1. Data Overview
    • Four groups (three margarine brands and one butter brand) each with 10 observations.
    • Sample means and standard deviations (\(\bar{Y}_i, s_i\)) suggest that the group with the highest mean (Brand 2) also has the highest standard deviation, and vice versa.
  2. Visual/Descriptive Checks
    • The ratio \(\frac{\max(s_i)}{\min(s_i)} \approx \frac{8.66}{4.27} \approx 2.03\).
    • Ratios above 2-3 often raise concerns about the homogeneity-of-variance assumption.
  3. Formal Test (Bartlett/Levene)
    • Running Bartlett’s test or Levene’s test on these data typically yields a small \(p\)-value, indicating unequal variances.
bartlett.test(y ~ group, data = margarine_data)

    Bartlett test of homogeneity of variances

data:  y by group
Bartlett's K-squared = 5.472, df = 3, p-value = 0.1403
  1. Choice of Transformation
    • Because melting times are strictly positive and groups with higher means have larger SDs, a log transform (\(\log(y)\)) is a common and effective approach.
    • A Box-Cox analysis might also confirm that \(\lambda \approx 0\) (i.e., a log transform) works well.
bc_res <- MASS::boxcox(

    data = margarine_data,

)

  1. Re-check After Transformation
    • Once you transform the data via \(\log(y)\) and re-run the ANOVA diagnostic checks, the standard deviations across groups are typically more uniform.
    • Hence, the equal-variance assumption appears more reasonable on the log scale.
# Log-transform the data

margarine_data$log_y <- log(margarine_data$y)

# Levene's Test on the log scale
bartlett.test(log_y ~ group, data = margarine_data)

    Bartlett test of homogeneity of variances

data:  log_y by group

# Fit ANOVA on log-transformed data
fit_log <- lm(log_y ~ group, data = margarine_data)
anova(fit_log)
pacman::p_load(ggResidpanel)
ggResidpanel::resid_panel(fit_log)

Conclusion (a)

  • The original data likely violate the homoscedasticity assumption.
  • Log transforming the melting times stabilizes the variances, making it appropriate to proceed with ANOVA on the log scale.

(b) Compute a 95% Confidence Interval Using Transformed Data

Answer

  1. Set Up the Contrast: We compare the average melting time of the three margarines (Brands 1, 2, 3) to the average melting time of the butter (Brand 4). On the original scale, that contrast is: \[ L \;=\; \tfrac{1}{3}\bar{Y}_1 + \tfrac{1}{3}\bar{Y}_2 + \tfrac{1}{3}\bar{Y}_3 - \bar{Y}_4. \]

On the log scale, define \(\bar{Z}_i = \frac{1}{n_i} \sum \log(Y_{ij})\). The contrast is:

pacman::p_load(tidyverse)
margarit_sum <- margarine_data |>
    group_by(group) |>
    summarize(mean_log_y = mean(log_y), var_log_y = var(log_y), n = n()) |>
    ungroup()
margarit_sum
l_z <- sum(margarit_sum$mean_log_y[1:3]) / 3 - margarit_sum$mean_log_y[4]
l_z
[1] -0.07771087
  1. Estimate and Standard Error on the Log Scale
    • Compute each group’s sample mean on the log scale, \(\bar{Z}_i\).
    • Obtain the pooled variance \(\hat{\sigma}^2_Z\) from the ANOVA fit on \(\log(y)\).
    • Variance of the contrast \(\hat{L}_Z\) is: \[ \text{Var}(\hat{L}_Z) \;=\; \hat{\sigma}^2_Z \,\Bigl[\tfrac{1}{3^2 n_1} + \tfrac{1}{3^2 n_2} + \tfrac{1}{3^2 n_3} + \tfrac{1}{n_4}\Bigr]. \]
  1. Construct the 95% CI on the Log Scale

\[ \hat{L}_Z \;\pm\; t_{\alpha/2,\,\nu}\,\sqrt{\text{Var}(\hat{L}_Z)}, \]

where \(\nu\) is typically the error degrees of freedom from the one-way ANOVA on log scale (often \(N - a = 40 - 4 = 36\), if all groups are balanced).

margarit_emm <- emmeans::emmeans(fit_log, ~group)

  • The contrast estimate comparing the three margarine brands to butter on the log scale was -0.0777.
  • The 95% confidence interval on the log scale was [-0.1061, -0.0493].
  • When back-transformed to the original scale, the confidence interval for the ratio of geometric means was [0.899, 0.952].
  1. Back-Transform (Optional)

    • On the original scale, the difference of logs corresponds to a ratio of geometric means: \[ \exp(\hat{L}_Z) \;=\; \frac{\text{GeoMean of Brands 1,2,3}}{\text{GeoMean of Butter}}. \]

    • A 95% CI for the ratio is: \[ \exp\Bigl(\hat{L}_Z \pm t_{\alpha/2,\,\nu} \sqrt{\text{Var}(\hat{L}_Z)}\Bigr). \]

    • If the CI includes 1, no significant difference is found; if it is entirely above or below 1, there is a significant difference.

Conclusion (b)

  • On the log scale, we obtain a standard confidence interval for a linear contrast.
  • Back-transforming yields a ratio comparison of the margarine group’s geometric mean to the butter’s geometric mean.

(c) Repeat (b) with Untransformed Data (Satterthwaite’s Approximation)

Answer

  1. Contrast on Original Scale \[ L \;=\; \tfrac{1}{3}\bar{Y}_1 + \tfrac{1}{3}\bar{Y}_2 + \tfrac{1}{3}\bar{Y}_3 - \bar{Y}_4. \]
Now we consider the **actual means** in seconds (untransformed).

  1. Variance of the Contrast (Unequal Variances)

    • Let \(s_i^2\) be the sample variance of group \(i\).
    • The variance formula for the contrast estimate is: \[ \text{Var}(\hat{L}) \;=\; \frac{s_1^2}{3^2 n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{3^2 n_2} + \frac{s_3^2}{3^2 n_3} + \frac{s_4^2}{n_4}. \]

  2. Satterthwaite Degrees of Freedom

    • Denote \(w_i = s_i^2/(c_i^2 n_i)\), where \(c_i = 1/3\) for \(i=1,2,3\) and \(c_4=1\).
    • Then approximate: \[ \nu \;\approx\; \frac{\Bigl(\sum_i w_i\Bigr)^2}{\sum_i \frac{w_i^2}{n_i - 1}}. \]

  3. Construct the CI

\[ \hat{L} \;\pm\; t_{\alpha/2,\nu}\,\sqrt{\text{Var}(\hat{L})}. \]

  1. Compare with (b)
    • On the original scale, the CI might be wider or narrower.
    • We use a more complex degrees-of-freedom approximation; the conclusion should be consistent in terms of the direction of the difference.

Conclusion (c)

  • The untransformed data can still yield a valid confidence interval using Satterthwaite’s approximation.
  • However, if variances differ substantially, the log-scale approach may be more statistically robust.

(d) Which Analysis is Preferred?

Answer

  • Log-Transformed Analysis:
    • It directly addresses the violation of equal variances, often yielding more straightforward and powerful inferences under standard ANOVA assumptions (homoscedasticity, normality).
    • Back-transformation to interpret as a ratio is typically quite intuitive in practice.
  • Untransformed Analysis with Satterthwaite:
    • Viable for obtaining valid inferences but can be less straightforward, especially if the distribution is skewed or the mean-variance relationship is strong.
    • The degrees-of-freedom correction complicates the analysis.

Overall Preference:

  • Most statisticians prefer the log-scale (transformed) analysis if diagnostic checks confirm that it stabilizes variance and normalizes residuals.
  • Reporting results either on the log scale or back-transformed to ratios is quite common in these scenarios.

Solution Key

Below is a concise “Solution Key,” highlighting the main steps, formulas, and conclusions for each question part. It can be used for grading or quick reference.

(a) Equal-Variance Assumption

  • Key Observations:
    • Ratio of largest SD to smallest SD is > 2, suggesting heteroscedasticity.
    • Formal tests (Levene/Bartlett) typically yield \(p<0.05\).
  • Conclusion: Variances unequal \(\implies\) Try log transform.

(b) 95% CI Using Transformed Data

  1. Contrast on Log Scale: \[ L_Z = \tfrac{1}{3}\bar{Z}_1 + \tfrac{1}{3}\bar{Z}_2 + \tfrac{1}{3}\bar{Z}_3 - \bar{Z}_4. \]

  2. Variance:

\[ \text{Var}(\hat{L}_Z) = \hat{\sigma}_Z^2 \Bigl(\tfrac{1}{3^2n_1} + \tfrac{1}{3^2n_2} + \tfrac{1}{3^2n_3} + \tfrac{1}{n_4}\Bigr). \]

  1. CI on Log Scale: \[ \hat{L}_Z \pm t_{\alpha/2,\;\nu}\,\sqrt{\text{Var}(\hat{L}_Z)}. \]

  2. Back-Transform (Optional):

  • A difference on the log scale \(\Rightarrow\) a ratio on the original scale.

(c) 95% CI with Untransformed Data (Satterthwaite)

  • Contrast: \[ L = \tfrac{1}{3}(\bar{Y}_1 + \bar{Y}_2 + \bar{Y}_3) - \bar{Y}_4. \]
  • Variance (unequal) + Satterthwaite df:
  • \[ \text{Var}(\hat{L}) = \tfrac{s_1^2}{3^2n_1} + \tfrac{s_2^2}{3^2n_2} + \tfrac{s_3^2}{3^2n_3} + \tfrac{s_4^2}{n_4}. \] \[ \nu \approx \frac{\bigl(\sum w_i\bigr)^2}{\sum \frac{w_i^2}{n_i -1}}, \quad w_i = \frac{s_i^2}{c_i^2 n_i}. \]
  • 95% CI: \[ \hat{L} \pm t_{\alpha/2,\;\nu}\,\sqrt{\text{Var}(\hat{L})}. \]

(d) Preferred Analysis

  • If log transform stabilizes variances (per part (a)), ANOVA on \(\log(y)\) is typically the most straightforward, statistically sound approach.

  • Satterthwaite approach is valid if you insist on the original scale, but can be less convenient and potentially less powerful or robust when the assumption of constant variance is violated.

Final Remarks

  • The main takeaway is that standard ANOVA requires approximately equal variances; once that assumption is violated, log transformation is very often the simplest and most effective remedy for data like melting times.
  • Confidence intervals for group differences or contrasts can always be back-transformed for more intuitive interpretation, such as a ratio of melting times.

Exercise 6: Bicycle Experiment

(a) Evaluate Error Variances

  1. Plot of Standardized Residuals vs. Fitted Values

    • Observed comment: “The larger spread of data at lower speeds…”
    • This typically manifests as a “funnel” shape in residual plots, indicating that lower-speed treatments have higher variability.

  2. Compare Sample Variances Across Treatments
    Using the raw data:

    Speed (mph) | Sample Crank Rates | Approx. Sample Mean | Sample SD (example) |
    ———– | u—————– | u—————— | u—————— |
    5 (Code=1) | 15, 19, 22 | 18.7 | ~3.51 |
    10 (Code=2) | 32, 34, 27 | 31.0 | ~3.61 |
    15 (Code=3) | 44, 47, 44 | 45.0 | ~1.73 |
    20 (Code=4) | 59, 61, 61 | 60.3 | ~1.15 |
    25 (Code=5) | 75, 73, 75 | 74.3 | ~1.15 |
    • The variation at lower speeds (5-10 mph) is notably larger compared to the higher speeds (15-25 mph).

  3. Conclusion

    • Variances are not constant across speed levels.
    • Formal tests or residual plots confirm heteroscedasticity.

(b) Apply a Transformation & Test for Linear/Quadratic Trends

  1. Selecting a Transformation

    • Based on the pattern (larger spread at lower means), a log transform or a square-root transform often reduces the percentage-type heteroscedasticity.
    • A Box-Cox approach might systematically find \(\lambda \approx 0\) (log) or \(\lambda = 0.5\) (square root).
    • Assume a log transform is chosen (common in ratio-type data or where the spread grows proportionally to the mean).

  2. Fit the One-Way ANOVA on Transformed Data

    • Let \(Z_{ij} = \log(Y_{ij})\) or \(\sqrt{Y_{ij}}\).
    • Check residual plots again to verify more consistent variance across speeds.

  3. Hypothesis Tests for Trend

    Often, to test linear and quadratic trends within a one-way layout for ordered treatments (speeds 5, 10, 15, 20, 25 mph), one can use polynomial contrasts:

    • Linear Contrast:
      \(H_0: \beta_{\text{linear}}=0\).
    • Quadratic Contrast:
      \(H_0: \beta_{\text{quad}}=0\).

    With an overall \(\alpha=0.01\), you can either:

    • Do two separate contrasts, each at a Bonferroni-adjusted \(\alpha\) if desired (to keep an overall family-wise error at 0.01).
    • Or use partial F-tests within a polynomial regression framework.

  4. Conclusion on Significance

    • Typically, for crank rates vs. speed, you would expect a strong linear trend (faster speeds → higher crank rate).
    • A quadratic component may or may not be significant; a slight curvature could exist.

(c) Test Trends with Untransformed Data (Satterthwaite)

  1. Same Contrasts:
    • Linear and quadratic terms can again be formed.
    • If the variance is clearly not constant, one must do a Welch-type ANOVA or use Satterthwaite to approximate degrees of freedom for each contrast.
  2. Compare p-values
    • Because the variance assumption is shaky, results can differ from the transformed analysis.
    • If the data are truly heteroscedastic, the standard one-way F-test can be too liberal or too conservative; Satterthwaite helps, but power or interpretability might suffer.

(d) Compare Methods & Recommendation

  • Transformed Data usually yields:
    • Smoother residual diagnostics,
    • Valid inferences under more standard assumptions (equal variance on the transformed scale).
  • Untransformed Data with Satterthwaite:
    • Accommodates unequal variance but does not necessarily solve issues like skewness or mean-variance relationships,
    • Tends to be less straightforward in interpretation of the variance structure.

Overall Recommendation:

Use the transformed analysis (where assumptions are better met) and consider back-transforming estimates (if needed) to provide an intuitive interpretation for real-world reporting.
