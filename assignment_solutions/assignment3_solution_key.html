1. Buoyancy Experiment

Step 1: Experimental Planning Checklist

  • (a) Define the objectives of the experiment
    • The primary objective is to examine how different salt concentrations in water affect the buoyancy of an object.
    • Expected outcome: A relationship between salinity and buoyant force.
  • (b) Identify sources of variation
    • Treatment Factors: Different levels of salt concentration.
    • Experimental Units: The objects being submerged.
    • Nuisance Factors: Temperature of the water, object shape, measurement inconsistencies.
  • (c) Assignment rule for experimental units
    • Randomized assignment of objects to different water samples with varying salt concentrations.
    • Replication of measurements to improve accuracy.
  • (d) Measurement and Procedures
    • Measuring the density of water with a hydrometer.
    • Measuring buoyant force by tracking object displacement or using a force sensor.
  • (e) Specify preplanned contrasts
    • Compare the mean buoyant force across different salt levels.
    • Trend analysis to determine if buoyancy increases linearly or follows a different pattern.
  • (f) Select multiple comparison methods
    • ANOVA for detecting significant differences.
    • Tukey’s HSD for post-hoc comparisons.

Solution Key and Grading Rubric for Cotton-Spinning Experiment

Background

The purpose of the experiment was twofold; first, to investigate the way in which different degrees of twist (measured in turns per inch) affected the breakage rate of the roving, and secondly, to compare the ordinary flyer with the newly devised special flyer.

  • The treatment factors are “type of flyer” and “degree of twist.”
  • The “type of flyer” factor has two levels: “ordinary” and “special”, coded as 1 and 2, respectively.
  • The “degree of twist” factor has four levels: 1.63, 1.69, 1.78, and 1.90 turns per inch.
  • The experiment was run with six treatment combinations: 12, 13, 14, 21, 22, and 23.
  • The two treatment combinations 11 and 24 were omitted from the experiment.

Key Experimental Variables

  • Block: The experimental blocking factor (to account for variation).
  • Treatment (Trtmt): The applied combination of Flyer and Twist. Not all combinations were used.
  • Flyer: The flyer type used in the spinning process (categorical: 1 for Ordinary, 2 for Special).
  • Twist: The level of twist applied to the yarn (continuous), measured in turns per inch. There are four levels.
  • Break: The breakage rate of the roving (response variable)

3. Meat Cooking Experiment (Continuation from Exercise 14, Chapter 3)

An experiment was run to investigate the amount of weight lost (in grams) by ground beef hamburgers after grilling or frying, and how much the weight loss is affected by the percentage fat in the beef before cooking. The experiment involved two factors: cooking method (factor A, with two levels frying and grilling, coded 1, 2), and fat content (factor B, with three levels 10, 15, and 20%, coded 1, 2, 3). Thus there were six treatment combinations 11, 12, 13, 21, 22, 23, relabeled as treatment levels 1, 2, …, 6, respectively. Hamburger patties weighing 110 g each were prepared from meat with the required fat content. There were 30 “cooking time slots” which were randomly assigned to the treatments in such a way that each treatment was observed five times (r = 5). Understanding the Structure of the Data

The Reaction Time Experiment data consists of observations measuring how quickly a participant responds to an auditory or visual cue. The study manipulates two factors:

  1. Cue Stimulus (Factor A): Auditory (A1) or Visual (A2).
  2. Elapsed Time (Factor B): 5 seconds (B1), 10 seconds (B2), or 15 seconds (B3).

Each treatment combination represents a specific pairing of these two factors:

Treatment Combination Cue Type Elapsed Time
1 Auditory (A1) 5 sec (B1)
2 Auditory (A1) 10 sec (B2)
3 Auditory (A1) 15 sec (B3)
4 Visual (A2) 5 sec (B1)
5 Visual (A2) 10 sec (B2)
6 Visual (A2) 15 sec (B3)

For each treatment combination, reaction times were recorded over multiple trials, with the order of data collection also noted.

Contrast Explanation

A contrast is a linear combination of treatment means that tests specific hypotheses about differences among treatments.

1. Main Effect of Cue Type (Auditory vs. Visual)

This contrast examines whether auditory cues lead to significantly different reaction times than visual cues. The contrast formula:

\[ C_1 = \frac{1}{3} (Y_{A1B1} + Y_{A1B2} + Y_{A1B3}) - \frac{1}{3} (Y_{A2B1} + Y_{A2B2} + Y_{A2B3}) \]

where:

  • \(Y_{A1B1}\) represents the mean reaction time for Auditory, 5s.
  • \(Y_{A1B2}\) represents the mean reaction time for Auditory, 10s.
  • \(Y_{A1B3}\) represents the mean reaction time for Auditory, 15s.
  • \(Y_{A2B1}\), \(Y_{A2B2}\), and \(Y_{A2B3}\) are the corresponding means for Visual cues.

This contrast has weights (+1/3, +1/3, +1/3, -1/3, -1/3, -1/3), which compare the average reaction time for auditory and visual cues.

2. Main Effect of Elapsed Time (Linear Trend)

This contrast tests whether increasing elapsed time affects reaction time linearly. The contrast formula:

\[ C_2 = (-1)Y_{B1} + 0 Y_{B2} + (+1) Y_{B3} \]

where \(Y_{B1}, Y_{B2}, Y_{B3}\) are the means across cue types at 5s, 10s, and 15s, respectively. The weights (-1, 0, +1) assume a linear increase or decrease in reaction time with elapsed time.

3. Interaction Between Cue Type and Elapsed Time

This contrast examines whether the effect of elapsed time on reaction time differs between auditory and visual cues. The formula:

\[ C_3 = (Y_{A1B1} - Y_{A1B3}) - (Y_{A2B1} - Y_{A2B3}) \]

This measures whether the difference between 5s and 15s is the same for auditory and visual cues. If this contrast is significant, it indicates an interaction effect, meaning the impact of elapsed time depends on the cue type.

Interpretation of Contrasts

  • If \(C_1\) is significant → Auditory cues lead to significantly different reaction times than visual cues.
  • If \(C_2\) is significant → Reaction time systematically changes as elapsed time increases.
  • If \(C_3\) is significant → The effect of elapsed time on reaction time is not the same for auditory and visual cues.— We do not discuss the interaction contrast in this assignment.

The simultaneous 90% confidence intervals for the contrasts are:

  1. Auditory vs. Visual: The difference in reaction times between auditory and visual cues is estimated to be between 0.165 and 0.269 seconds.
  2. Linear Trend: The linear trend in reaction times across elapsed times is estimated to be between -0.019 and 0.066 seconds.
  3. Interpretation: The confidence intervals suggest that visual cues lead to significantly faster reaction times than auditory cues. However, the linear trend in reaction times across elapsed times is not statistically significant.
