Below is a self-contained classroom handout illustrating step-by-step how to set up and analyze a Latin Square design, inspired by the ideas in Box, Hunter, and Hunter (“Statistics for Experimenters”) regarding experiments that must block on two factors in addition to comparing several treatments.

1. Context and Purpose

We want to compare four teaching methods (labeled A, B, C, D) for their effect on students’ test scores. However, the teacher suspects that two other factors also cause variability:

  1. Teacher differences. Four different teachers (labeled T1, T2, T3, T4) may have different baseline abilities or styles.
  2. Classroom differences. Four classrooms (labeled R1, R2, R3, R4) may differ in environment, resources, or size.

We would like to “block” on both Teachers and Classrooms so that the primary comparison (A vs B vs C vs D) is not distorted by large differences among teachers or classrooms. A Latin Square layout ensures each treatment is tested once per teacher and once per classroom.

2. Layout of a 4×4 Latin Square

A 4×4 Latin Square ensures:

  • Each row represents one teacher (T1, T2, T3, T4).
  • Each column represents one classroom (R1, R2, R3, R4).
  • Each of the four treatments (A, B, C, D) appears exactly once in every row and once in every column.

An example random assignment is shown in the table below. (In practice, the row and column labels themselves—and the letter labels for the treatments—are also randomized.)

R1 R2 R3 R4
T1 A B C D
T2 B C D A
T3 C D A B
T4 D A B C

Randomization Note: - We randomly label the rows (teachers T1–T4). - We randomly label the columns (classrooms R1–R4). - We randomly assign the letters (A, B, C, D) to the four teaching methods. - Then we fill the 4×4 grid so that each letter appears exactly once per row and once per column.

3. Example Data

Suppose after implementing the above plan, the student test scores (averaged if multiple students per cell) turn out as follows:

R1 R2 R3 R4 Row Means (Teacher)
T1 (A, B, C, D) 82 88 85 83 84.5
T2 (B, C, D, A) 79 87 81 80 81.8
T3 (C, D, A, B) 84 89 82 86 85.3
T4 (D, A, B, C) 81 78 80 83 80.5
Column Means (Classroom) 81.5 85.5 82.0 83.0 -

From this table, each entry is “the average test score” for that Teacher–Classroom combination under the assigned teaching method. We can gather the raw data into a single 16–observation list if we wish, but it is easiest to keep it in this 4×4 grid.

Let’s label these responses by \(Y_{ij}\), where \(i\) indicates the teacher (row) and \(j\) indicates the classroom (column). Our grand average \(\bar{Y}\) is the mean of all 16 scores.

4. Model Assumptions

A Latin Square experiment typically uses an additive model:

\[ Y_{ij} \;=\; \mu \;+\; \theta_i \;+\; \phi_j \;+\; \tau_{k(i,j)} \;+\; \varepsilon_{ij} \]

  • \(\mu\) = overall mean
  • \(\theta_i\) = effect of row \(i\) (e.g., teacher \(i\))
  • \(\phi_j\) = effect of column \(j\) (e.g., classroom \(j\))
  • \(\tau_{k(i,j)}\) = effect of whichever treatment \(k\) is assigned in row \(i\), column \(j\)
  • \(\varepsilon_{ij}\) = residual (“noise”) term

The design ensures each treatment \(\tau_k\) is replicated four times overall (once in each row and once in each column).

Key assumption: There is no strong interaction among teacher, classroom, and the treatment. In other words, the teacher and classroom merely “add” or “subtract” some baseline amount from the outcome. If strong interactions are suspected, a Latin Square is not appropriate.

5. The Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)

We decompose total variation into three parts:

  1. Differences among rows (teachers).
  2. Differences among columns (classrooms).
  3. Differences among treatments (the four methods).
  4. Residual “error” (everything left over).

5.1 Step-by-Step Sums of Squares

  1. Compute Grand Average (\(\bar{Y}\)):

\[ \bar{Y} \;=\; \frac{\text{sum of all 16 observations}}{16}. \]

From the table above, adding all 16 values:

\[ 82 + 88 + 85 + 83 \;+\; 79 + 87 + 81 + 80 \;+\; 84 + 89 + 82 + 86 \;+\; 81 + 78 + 80 + 83 \;=\; 1278. \]

Hence,

\[ \bar{Y} = \frac{1278}{16} = 79.875. \]

(Rounded as needed; we’ll carry decimals through for demonstration.)

  1. Row (Teacher) Means: We already have row means from the table, e.g. for T1,

\[ \bar{Y}_{\text{row }1} \;=\; \frac{82 + 88 + 85 + 83}{4} = 84.5. \]

Similarly for T2, T3, T4.

  1. Column (Classroom) Means: We also have them from the table, e.g. for R1,

\[ \bar{Y}_{\text{col }1} = \frac{82 + 79 + 84 + 81}{4} = 81.5. \]

  1. Treatment Means: Gather the four observations corresponding to each treatment (A, B, C, D). For example, treatment A appears in (T1,R1), (T2,R4), (T3,R3), (T4,R2) in our table. Compute its mean from those 4 data points. Repeat for B, C, D.

  2. Sums of Squares:

    • Total Sum of Squares (SST), relative to the grand mean:

\[ \text{SST} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^4 \sum_{j=1}^4 \left(Y_{ij} - \bar{Y}\right)^2. \]

  • Row (Teacher) Sum of Squares:

\[ \text{SS}_{\text{Rows}} \;=\; 4 \,\sum_{i=1}^4 \left(\bar{Y}_{\text{row } i} - \bar{Y}\right)^2. \]

 (Multiply by 4 because each row mean is based on 4 observations.)
  • Column (Classroom) Sum of Squares:

\[ \text{SS}_{\text{Columns}} \;=\; 4 \,\sum_{j=1}^4 \left(\bar{Y}_{\text{col } j} - \bar{Y}\right)^2. \]

  • Treatment Sum of Squares:

\[ \text{SS}_{\text{Treat}} \;=\; 4 \,\sum_{k=A,B,C,D} \left(\bar{Y}_{k} - \bar{Y}\right)^2. \]

 (Again, each treatment mean is based on 4 observations in a 4×4 square.)
  • Residual Sum of Squares:

\[ \text{SS}_{\text{Error}} \;=\; \text{SST} \;-\; \text{SS}_{\text{Rows}} \;-\; \text{SS}_{\text{Columns}} \;-\; \text{SS}_{\text{Treat}}. \]

5.2 Degrees of Freedom

  • Total: \(16 - 1 = 15\).
  • Rows: \(4 - 1 = 3\).
  • Columns: \(4 - 1 = 3\).
  • Treatments: \(4 - 1 = 3\).
  • Residual: \(15 - (3+3+3) = 6\).

5.3 Mean Squares and the F-Ratios

  • Mean Square for Rows:

\[ \text{MS}_{\text{Rows}} = \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{Rows}}}{3}. \]

  • Mean Square for Columns:

\[ \text{MS}_{\text{Columns}} = \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{Columns}}}{3}. \]

  • Mean Square for Treatments:

\[ \text{MS}_{\text{Treat}} = \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{Treat}}}{3}. \]

  • Residual Mean Square:

\[ \text{MS}_{\text{Error}} = \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{Error}}}{6}. \]

We then test for significance:

  • Rows vs. Error: \(F_{\text{Rows}} = \frac{\text{MS}_{\text{Rows}}}{\text{MS}_{\text{Error}}}\).
  • Columns vs. Error: \(F_{\text{Columns}} = \frac{\text{MS}_{\text{Columns}}}{\text{MS}_{\text{Error}}}\).
  • Treatments vs. Error: \(F_{\text{Treat}} = \frac{\text{MS}_{\text{Treat}}}{\text{MS}_{\text{Error}}}\).

Each ratio follows (approximately) an \(F\)-distribution with appropriate degrees of freedom (numerator df = 3, denominator df = 6 in each case), so we can check standard \(F\) tables or software to see if the ratio is large enough to claim statistical significance.

6. Illustrative (Partial) ANOVA Table

Below is a partial example of how an ANOVA table might look. (Numerical values are for demonstration only.)

Source SS df MS F-Ratio p-Value
Rows (T1–T4) 72.5 3 24.17 4.12 0.05
Columns (R1–R4) 60.3 3 20.10 3.43 0.08
Treatments (A,B,C,D) 90.0 3 30.00 5.12 0.03
Residual 105.0 6 17.50 – –
Total 327.8 15 – – –
  • We see that Treatments might be significant at around the 3% level, meaning there is evidence that at least one method (A, B, C, or D) differs from the others.
  • Rows (teachers) may also be significant at around 5%.
  • Columns (classrooms) are not as strongly significant but still borderline.
  • The conclusion might be that the teaching methods do affect student test scores, with teacher differences also playing a role.

7. Graphical ANOVA (Optional)

To complement the ANOVA table, we can create a Graphical ANOVA:

  1. Plot the row effects (teacher means minus grand mean) on a dot chart scaled so that the “noise” is visually comparable.
  2. Plot the column effects (classroom means minus grand mean).
  3. Plot the treatment effects (method means minus grand mean).
  4. Plot the residuals.

If the spread of points for “Treatments” is obviously bigger than the residual spread, that visually supports that treatments matter. Meanwhile, if row or column spreads are large, that tells us there really are teacher or classroom blocks that differ.

8. Practical Advice and Interpretation

  1. Check Residuals: Make sure there are no severe outliers or strong patterns that hint at non-constant variance or a need for transformation (e.g. log-transform).
  2. Check Interactions: A Latin Square works best when row and column factors do not strongly interact with the treatments. If you see suspicious patterns, consider alternative designs.
  3. Replications: Often, people replicate the entire Latin Square more than once if they can afford extra runs. That increases residual degrees of freedom.
  4. Conclusions: If the “Treatments” \(F\)-ratio is large (small p-value), we conclude at least one of the methods differs significantly. Follow-up comparisons (like pairwise t-tests with error MS) can clarify which specific method is better.

9. Step-by-Step Summary for Students

  1. Plan and Randomize
    • Assign each “row” factor (Teacher T1–T4) at random.
    • Assign each “column” factor (Classroom R1–R4) at random.
    • Randomly assign treatments (A, B, C, D) so each appears once per row/column.
  2. Collect Data
    • Fill in the 4×4 data grid with the response measured in each cell.
  3. Compute Averages
    • Grand Mean = sum of all 16 responses / 16.
    • Row Means = average for each teacher.
    • Column Means = average for each classroom.
    • Treatment Means = average across the 4 cells that use that treatment.
  4. ANOVA Calculations
    • Total SS: sum of squared deviations from grand mean.
    • Row SS: multiply 4 by the sum of squared deviations of row means from grand mean.
    • Column SS: multiply 4 by sum of squared deviations of column means from grand mean.
    • Treatment SS: multiply 4 by sum of squared deviations of treatment means from grand mean.
    • Residual SS: difference = Total SS–(Row + Column + Treatment SS).
  5. Degrees of Freedom
    • Total df = 16–1 = 15.
    • Each of the row, column, and treatment sources has 3 df.
    • Residual df = 15–(3+3+3) = 6.
  6. Mean Squares and F-Tests
    • MS = SS / df.
    • \(F = \frac{\text{MS}_{\text{Factor}}}{\text{MS}_{\text{Error}}}\).
    • Compare each \(F\)-value to the \(F\)-distribution with (3,6) df to see if it is large enough to be significant.
  7. Interpret Results
    • Identify whether row or column blocking was significant.
    • Check if treatments differ significantly.
    • Present a final conclusion and any potential next steps (e.g., further studies, transformations, or different design if strong interactions are suspected).

10. Final Remarks

A Latin Square design:

  • Blocks out two known sources of variation (rows and columns).
  • Tests a single primary factor of interest (the treatment).
  • Is valid under randomization (rows, columns, and treatments all randomized).
  • Is straightforward to analyze with a standard ANOVA and standard software.

This example helps students see:

  1. How to construct a Latin Square.
  2. Why it is effective at removing large, unwanted variability in two directions (rows & columns).
  3. How to perform step-by-step calculations of the sums of squares, degrees of freedom, and F-ratios.
