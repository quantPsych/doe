12.6 Video Game Experiment

Data Source: Dean et al. (2017), Chapter 12, Exercise 6

Professor Robert Wardrop conducted an experiment in 1991 to evaluate which of five sound modes helped him play a certain video game best. The sound modes included:

  • Modes 1–3: Three different types of background music with game sounds expected to enhance play.
  • Mode 4: Game sounds only, no background music.
  • Mode 5: No music or game sounds.

The experiment used a Latin square design with two blocking factors: day and time order of the game. The response variable was the game score (higher scores indicate better performance). The design and data are provided in Table 12.16.

Questions

  1. Write down a possible model for the data and check the model assumptions. If the assumptions appear to be approximately satisfied, proceed with parts (b)–(f).

  2. Plot the adjusted data and discuss the plot.

  3. Complete an analysis of variance table.

  4. Evaluate whether blocking (day and time order) was effective.

  5. Construct simultaneous 95% confidence intervals for:

    1. All pairwise comparisons of the five sound modes.
    2. The “music versus no music” contrast:

\[ \frac{1}{3}(\tau_1 + \tau_2 + \tau_3) - \frac{1}{2}(\tau_4 + \tau_5) \] 3. The “game sound versus no game sound” contrast:

\[ \frac{1}{4}(\tau_1 + \tau_2 + \tau_3 + \tau_4) - \tau_5 \]

  1. Summarize the conclusions of the experiment. Which sound mode(s) should Professor Wardrop use for optimal performance?

12.10 Quantity Perception Experiment

Data Source: Dean et al. (2017), Chapter 12, Exercise 10

An experiment conducted in 1996 investigated how color influences the perception of quantity. Subjects were asked to guess the number of candies on a tray after viewing it for 3 seconds. The response was the difference between the actual number of candies and the guessed number.

  • Factors:
    • Number of candies (levels): 17, 29, 41
    • Color (levels): Yellow, Orange, Brown

Each subject viewed all nine treatment combinations based on a \(9 \times 9\) Latin square. The data provided in Table 12.18 represents a 2-replicate Latin square.

  • Treatment Combinations Coding:
    • \(1 = (17, \text{Yellow})\), \(2 = (17, \text{Orange})\), \(3 = (17, \text{Brown})\)
    • \(4 = (29, \text{Yellow})\), \(5 = (29, \text{Orange})\), \(6 = (29, \text{Brown})\)
    • \(7 = (41, \text{Yellow})\), \(8 = (41, \text{Orange})\), \(9 = (41, \text{Brown})\)

Questions

  1. Discuss whether the subjects in this study are likely to represent a larger population of interest. Are the conclusions relevant to the general population of noncolorblind individuals?

  2. Fit a model including the effects of:

  • Two blocking factors (Subject and Time Order)
  • The treatment effect
  • The treatment × time-order interaction.

Verify that the interaction (adjusted for subjects) can be measured with \((v-1)^2 = 64\) degrees of freedom.

  1. Examine whether the residuals are approximately normally distributed and whether variances are homogeneous across treatments.

    • Consider transformations of the response variable, such as:
      1. Original response: “Guessed number”
      2. “Square root of guessed number”
      3. “(True number − guessed number) / (True number)”
      4. Other appropriate transformations.

  2. Present an analysis of variance table and test any relevant hypotheses. Summarize your conclusions.

  3. Rewrite the model in terms of main effects and interactions of the two treatment factors (Number and Color). Redo the analysis of variance and interpret the results.

  4. Propose a plan for a follow-up experiment, including a checklist of key considerations.
