Data Source: Dean et al. (2017), Chapter 12, Exercise 2

  1. Show that there is only one standard \(3 \times 3\) Latin square.

12.6 Video Game Experiment

Data Source: Dean et al. (2017), Chapter 12, Exercise 6

Professor Robert Wardrop conducted an experiment in 1991 to evaluate which of five sound modes helped him play a certain video game best. The sound modes included:

  • Modes 1–3: Three different types of background music with game sounds expected to enhance play.
  • Mode 4: Game sounds only, no background music.
  • Mode 5: No music or game sounds.

The experiment used a Latin square design with two blocking factors: day and time order of the game. The response variable was the game score (higher scores indicate better performance). The design and data are provided in Table 12.16.

Latin square design showing treatments and data for the video game experiment
Time Order Day 1 Day 2 Day 3 Day 4 Day 5
1 1 (94) 3 (100) 4 (98) 2 (101) 5 (112)
2 3 (103) 2 (111) 1 (51) 5 (110) 4 (90)
3 4 (114) 1 (75) 5 (94) 3 (85) 2 (107)
4 5 (100) 4 (74) 2 (70) 1 (93) 3 (106)
5 2 (106) 5 (95) 3 (81) 4 (90) 1 (73)

Questions

  1. Write down a possible model for the data and check the model assumptions. If the assumptions appear to be approximately satisfied, proceed with parts (b)–(f).

  2. Plot the adjusted data and discuss the plot.

  3. Complete an analysis of variance table.

  4. Evaluate whether blocking (day and time order) was effective.

  5. Construct simultaneous 95% confidence intervals for:

    1. All pairwise comparisons of the five sound modes.
    2. The “music versus no music” contrast:

\[ \frac{1}{3}(\tau_1 + \tau_2 + \tau_3) - \frac{1}{2}(\tau_4 + \tau_5) \] 3. The “game sound versus no game sound” contrast:

\[ \frac{1}{4}(\tau_1 + \tau_2 + \tau_3 + \tau_4) - \tau_5 \]

  1. Summarize the conclusions of the experiment. Which sound mode(s) should Professor Wardrop use for optimal performance?
