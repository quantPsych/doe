Instructions

  1. Objective: Perform multiple comparisons and contrasts for experimental data, including Scheffé’s method and simultaneous confidence intervals.
  2. Use R or a similar tool to conduct the analyses for each exercise. Submit your results, including code and explanations, in a clear format.
  3. Clearly explain your analysis process for full credit.
  4. Submit your assignment as a single PDF file.

Total Points: 100

1. Buoyancy Experiment

Investigate the question: “Is the buoyancy of an object in water affected by different concentrations of salt in the water?”

Tasks: (a) Address steps (a)–(d) from the experimental planning checklist in detail:

  • Define the objectives of the experiment.
  • Identify sources of variation, including treatment factors, experimental units, and nuisance factors.
  • Choose an assignment rule for the experimental units.
  • Specify measurements, procedures, and any anticipated difficulties.
  • Specify preplanned contrasts or functions to estimate. Justify your choices.
  • Determine which multiple comparison methods, if any, are appropriate and explain why.

  1. Conduct a small pilot experiment to estimate the preliminary variance (\(\sigma^2\)). Use R to simulate data if needed and summarize findings.

  2. Revisit and complete the checklist by evaluating findings from the pilot study and revising experimental decisions if necessary.

3. Meat Cooking Experiment (Continuation from Exercise 14, Chapter 3)

An experiment was run to investigate the amount of weight lost (in grams) by ground beef ham- burgers after grilling or frying, and how much the weight loss is affected by the percentage fat in the beef before cooking. The experiment involved two factors: cooking method (factor A, with two levels frying and grilling, coded 1, 2), and fat content (factor B, with three levels 10, 15, and 20%, coded 1, 2, 3). Thus there were six treatment combinations 11, 12, 13, 21, 22, 23, relabeled as treatment levels 1, 2, …, 6, respectively. Hamburger patties weighing 110 g each were prepared from meat with the required fat content. There were 30 “cooking time slots” which were randomly assigned to the treatments in such a way that each treatment was observed five times (r = 5). The patty weights after cooking are shown in Table 3.14.

  1. Conduct pairwise comparisons for the six treatments using Scheffé’s method with a 95% overall confidence level. Present your results with appropriate statistical summaries and visualizations.

  2. Analyze the expressions \(\mu + \frac{(\tau_1 + \tau_4)}{2}\), \(\mu + \frac{(\tau_2 + \tau_5)}{2}\), and \(\mu + \frac{(\tau_3 + \tau_6)}{2}\):

  • Interpret what these expressions represent in the context of the experimental setup.
  • Perform pairwise comparisons among these three treatment averages using Scheffé’s method with a 95% confidence level.
  • Summarize and interpret the findings in terms of practical significance and experimental insights.

4. Reaction Time Experiment

Source: (L. Cai, T. Li, Nishant, and A. van der Kouwe, 1996)

This experiment investigates the effects of auditory and visual cues on the speed of response of a human subject. A personal computer presents a stimulus to the subject, and the reaction time required to press a key is monitored. The subject is warned of the forthcoming stimulus through auditory or visual cues. The study also considers different elapsed times between cue and stimulus as a factor.

Factors and Levels:

  • Cue Stimulus: Auditory or Visual
  • Elapsed Time: 5, 10, or 15 seconds

Treatment Combinations and Codes:

  • 1 = Auditory, 5 seconds
  • 2 = Auditory, 10 seconds
  • 3 = Auditory, 15 seconds
  • 4 = Visual, 5 seconds
  • 5 = Visual, 10 seconds
  • 6 = Visual, 15 seconds

The pilot experiment data, involving one subject, are shown in Table 4.4.

Understanding the Structure of the Data

The Reaction Time Experiment data consists of observations measuring how quickly a participant responds to an auditory or visual cue. The study manipulates two factors:

  1. Cue Stimulus (Factor A): Auditory (A1) or Visual (A2).
  2. Elapsed Time (Factor B): 5 seconds (B1), 10 seconds (B2), or 15 seconds (B3).

Each treatment combination represents a specific pairing of these two factors:

Treatment Combination Cue Type Elapsed Time
1 Auditory (A1) 5 sec (B1)
2 Auditory (A1) 10 sec (B2)
3 Auditory (A1) 15 sec (B3)
4 Visual (A2) 5 sec (B1)
5 Visual (A2) 10 sec (B2)
6 Visual (A2) 15 sec (B3)

For each treatment combination, reaction times were recorded over multiple trials, with the order of data collection also noted.

Tasks: (a) Identify Preplanned Contrasts:

  • Determine contrasts of interest, such as comparing auditory versus visual treatments. Specify the contrasts mathematically, including coefficients for the comparisons.
  1. Plot the Data:
  • Visualize the reaction times using an appropriate plot (e.g., bar or line plot). Summarize what the plot reveals about the treatment effects.
  1. Hypothesis Testing:
  • Test the null hypothesis \(H_0\): The treatments have no effect on reaction time against the alternative \(H_1\): The treatments have an effect on reaction time. Include a detailed explanation of the statistical test used and the results obtained.
  1. Simultaneous Confidence Intervals:
  • Compute simultaneous 90% confidence intervals for the preplanned contrasts using a chosen method (e.g., Tukey’s or Scheffé’s method). Interpret these intervals and state your conclusions about the treatment effects.
