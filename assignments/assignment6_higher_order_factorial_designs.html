Instructions

  1. Objective: Explore higher-order factorial designs and interactions in experimental data.
  2. Use R to conduct the analyses for each exercise. Submit your results, including code and explanations, in a clear format.
  3. Clearly explain your analysis process for full credit.
  4. Submit your assignment as a single PDF file.

Total points: 140

Exercise 7.7: Coating Experiment

This exercise examines the effects of spray parameters on the thermal spray coating properties of alumina (\(\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3\)). The experimental factors and levels are as follows:

  • Fuel ratio (A): 1:2.8 and 1:2.0
  • Carrier gas flow rate (B): 1.33 and 3.21 \(\text{L/s}^{-1}\)
  • Frequency of detonations (C): 2 and 4 Hz
  • Spray distance (D): 180 and 220 mm

The response variable is porosity (vol. %). Data are shown in Table 7.19.

Questions:

  1. Design an Analysis

Assuming negligible threeand four-factor interactions, outline the steps required to analyze the data (refer to Step (g) of the checklist in Chapter 2).

  1. Check Model Assumptions

Evaluate whether the assumptions underlying the model are valid.

  1. Perform the Analysis

Conduct the analysis you outlined in part (1), including interaction plots if appropriate. Clearly state your conclusions.

Exercise 7.11: Antifungal Antibiotic Experiment

This exercise investigates the effects of incubation conditions on the yield of an antifungal antibiotic.

  • Factors and Levels:
  • Incubation temperature (A): 25, 30, and 37 \(^\circ \text{C}\)
  • Carbon concentration (B): 2%, 5%, and 7.5%
  • Nitrogen concentration (C): 0.5%, 1%, and 3%

The response variable is the antifungal yield (measured in activity against Candida albicans). Data are shown in Table 7.23.

Questions:

  1. Assess Main Effects

Construct plots to assess the significance of main effects of \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\) on the response. What are your conclusions?

  1. Interaction Assumptions

State the assumptions you made regarding interactions while analyzing main effects in part (1).

  1. Two-Way Interactions

Construct plots to assess the significance of two-way interactions. Do they alter your conclusions from part (1)?

  1. Fit a Model

Assuming no three-way interaction, fit a model with all main effects and two-way interactions. Discuss the significance of the effects and compare with your conclusions from part (3).

  1. Model Diagnostics

Evaluate whether the assumptions of normality and equal error variances are satisfied. Identify potential outliers.

Exercise 7.12: Antifungal Antibiotic Experiment (Extended)

For this exercise, consider the data from Table 7.23 but assume no negligible three-factor interaction. Modify levels of \(A\) and \(B\) so their third levels are 35 and 8, respectively, to ensure equal spacing.

Questions:

  1. Trend Contrasts

Create a table listing the 27 treatment combinations. Include contrast coefficients for:

  • Linear and quadratic trends in \(A\) and \(B\)
  • Interaction trends (\(A \times B\): Linear-Linear, Linear-Quadratic, etc.)
  1. Normalize Contrasts

Identify divisors to normalize each contrast and manually compute least squares estimates for normalized Linear \(A\) and Quadratic \(A\) contrasts.

  1. Orthogonal Contrasts for \(C\)

Propose two orthogonal contrasts for \(C\) to address its uneven spacing and add them to the table in part (1).

  1. Orthogonal Contrasts Analysis

Use software to calculate least squares estimates for all 26 orthogonal contrasts. Generate a half-normal probability plot and interpret the results.

  1. Alternative Analysis

Apply the Voss and Wang method to analyze the orthogonal contrasts. Compare these findings to part (4).
