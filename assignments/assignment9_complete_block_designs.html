Effervescent Experiment

Data Source: Dean et al. (2017), Chapter 10, Exercise 13

The effervescent experiment, conducted by B. Bailey, J. Lewis, J. Speiser, Z. Thomas, and S. White (2011), aimed to compare the dissolving times of two brands of cold medicine tablets in different water temperatures.

  • Factors and Levels:
    • Brand: 1 (Name Brand), 2 (Store Brand)
    • Water Temperature: 1 (6°C), 2 (23°C), 3 (40°C)
  • Design: A complete block design with:
    • Blocks:
      • Block I: Stirred liquid at 350 RPM using a magnetic stirring plate.
      • Block II: Unstirred liquid.
    • Observations: Four per treatment combination, with a total of six treatment combinations.
    • Each observation represents an average of times measured by four experimenters.
  • Response Variable: Dissolving time in seconds.

Questions

  1. Plot and Explore the Data: Create appropriate plots to visualize the dissolving time for each treatment combination, such as interaction plots, boxplots, or scatterplots. Comment on any patterns, anomalies, or interesting features observed in the data.

  2. Fit a Block-Treatment Model:

  • Fit a linear model with block and treatment factors.
  • Examine residuals to check assumptions, including outlier detection, variance equality, and normality of residuals.
  • Report and interpret results.
  1. Analyze Temperature Effects:
  • Perform pairwise comparisons between the three temperature levels using appropriate post-hoc tests.
  • Test for linear and quadratic trends in temperature effects on dissolving time, assuming these trends were pre-planned in the experimental design.

Insole Cushion Experiment

Data Source: Dean et al. (2017), Chapter 10, Exercise 15

This experiment, conducted in 1995 by V. Agresti, S. Decker, T. Karakostas, E. Patterson, and S. Schwartz, investigated how different insole cushions and brands affect the vertical ground reaction force.

  • Factors and Levels:
    • Cushion Type (C): 1 (Regular), 2 (Heel)
    • Brand (D): 1 (Name Brand), 2 (Store Brand)
  • Design:
    • Single participant, alternating between dominant (right) and non-dominant (left) legs over two blocks (days).
    • Each of the four treatment combinations measured five times in randomized order per block.
  • Response Variable: Maximum deceleration of the vertical ground reaction force (in Newtons).

Yeast Experiment

Data Source: Dean et al. (2017), Chapter 10, Exercise 16

This experiment explored the effects of yeast type, initial temperature, and water bath temperature on the rate of yeast rising.

  • Factors and Levels:
    • Initial Water Temperature (C): 100°F, 115°F, 130°F
    • Yeast Type (D): 1 (Rapid Rise), 2 (Regular)
    • Bath Temperature (E): 70°F, 85°F
  • Design:
    • Three blocks, with each experimenter-team forming a block.
    • Each treatment combination observed twice per block.
  • Response Variable: Percentage gain in height after 15 minutes.

Questions

  1. Randomization Justification: Explain why treatment combinations should be randomized within each block before measurements.

  2. Residual Normality:

  • Discuss the general importance of checking residual normality.
  • Explain its relevance to this experiment’s context.
  1. Sample Size for Precision:
  • Calculate the minimum number of observations per treatment combination required to achieve a 99% confidence interval with a half-width of ≤5%. Use an error variance estimate of 9 percent².
  1. ANOVA Table:
  • Construct an ANOVA table for the data.
  • Discuss the significance of main effects, interactions, and their implications.
  1. Visualize Treatment Effects:
  • Create two plots to illustrate the main points about treatment effects. Justify the choice of these plots.
  1. Trend Analysis:
  • Test for a significant linear trend in percentage height gain as initial water temperature increases. Use a significance level of 0.01 and state conclusions.
